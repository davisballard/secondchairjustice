### 1. KLIM TYPE FOUNDRY — klim.co.nz
**New Zealand. Est. 2005. Designer: Kris Sowersby.**
**Principal Typefaces: Tiempos, Söhne, Founders Grotesk, National 2, Martina Plantijn, Calibre**

#### Site Visual Language
The Klim site is pure restraint. White ground, black text, no photography, no illustration. Navigation is minimal. The typefaces sell themselves by being set in themselves. There is no visual noise — only type and white space. The hierarchy is strict: font name, followed by specimen, followed by "in use" examples showing real-world application. The design argument is: *if your typefaces are this good, the website doesn't need anything else.*

#### Color System
White (#FFFFFF) ground. Near-black text. No brand color. The absence of color is itself a statement — color would dilute the typographic authority.

#### Typography
The site is set in Klim's own typefaces — primarily Founders Grotesk for navigation and UI, with specimens displayed in the relevant face being showcased. The effect is a perpetual live demonstration.

#### Layout Principles
- Single-column or simple grid; generous margins
- No decoration, no illustration, no photography
- Specimens presented at scale — type is the image
- "In use" section shows actual client deployments, functioning as portfolio through typographic work rather than agency showreel

#### The Design Philosophy (From Their Own Writing)
Klim's blog posts on each typeface are scholarly: "Die Grotesk was shaped in the long shadow of Helvetica, a typeface both revered and resented in equal measure." "Martina Plantijn is a better Plantin." "The Future is a homage to Futura." The through-line: **every Klim release is a historical typeface, reexamined through rigorous research, made decisively better for contemporary use.** They don't invent from scratch — they improve from deep knowledge.

**Active typefaces of note:**
- **Tiempos Headline/Text** — "A modern serif for editorial typography." Updates Plantin/Times for contemporary use. Warm curves + sharp details. Used by Cambridge Audio, Université Catholique de Lille, Puresport, Prāvo (a legal brand).
- **Söhne** — An Akzidenz Grotesk/Helvetica heir with more warmth and optical precision. The restrained grotesque.
- **Founders Grotesk** — Franklin Gothic lineage, cleaned up. Workhorse sans with American newspaper authority.
- **Martina Plantijn** — "A better Plantin." 16th century letterpress ancestry, decisively updated for digital production.
- **Die Grotesk** — A Helvetica examination — neither reverence nor parody, but a genuine reckoning.

#### Register
Scholarly. Craft-forward. Speaks to designers who know their type history. Communicates that typefaces are intellectual artifacts, not software products.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
**Yes — definitively.** Every release is rooted in historical precedent and brought forward with contemporary precision. This is the core Klim thesis: history is not a constraint but a resource.

#### Relevant to Second Chair
Tiempos Headline is a direct Second Chair candidate — it communicates editorial seriousness without stuffiness. The "warm curves + sharp details" balance is exactly the register Second Chair needs: authoritative but not cold, institutional but not dead. Tiempos has been used for a legal brand (Prāvo) and a Catholic university — both institutional contexts requiring dignity.

### 2. FRERE-JONES TYPE — frerejones.com
**New York. Est. 2014. Designer: Tobias Frere-Jones (formerly of Hoefler&Co).**
**Principal Typefaces: Mallory, Retina, Conductor, Empirica, Exchange**

#### Site Visual Language
The Frere-Jones site is spare and precise — a direct extension of Tobias Frere-Jones's design philosophy, which prizes invisible craft. The site was partially restricted during research, but the foundry's aesthetic is well-documented through its work: white space, strict hierarchy, specimens that prioritize legibility demonstration over spectacle.

#### The Design Philosophy
Frere-Jones is perhaps the most academically rigorous type designer working today. His typefaces are designed around specific optical problems: Retina was commissioned by the Wall Street Journal and designed to survive at 7pt in newsprint. Mallory was built to occupy the specific register between Gill Sans's humanity and Helvetica's neutrality — institutional without coldness. Empirica was designed for the institutional contexts of government and large organizations.

**Active typefaces of note:**
- **Mallory** — Humanist sans occupying the register between Gill Sans and Helvetica. Warm enough to feel human, neutral enough for institutions. The face of serious organizations that don't want to be cold.
- **Retina** — Hyper-legible serif engineered for small sizes in low-resolution conditions. Originally for financial data. Now used across media that prizes legibility over decoration.
- **Conductor** — Extended condensed display typeface. Strong vertical presence. Headline authority without heaviness.
- **Exchange** — Text serif for dense editorial environments. Highly optimized for print body copy.

#### Register
Hyper-technical. The design equivalent of a precision instrument manufacturer. Speaks to typographers who understand that craft lives in the details most people cannot see.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
**Yes — but more functional than aesthetic.** Frere-Jones synthesizes historical optical principles (the solutions discovered by punchcutters working at physical scale) with digital precision. The result is typefaces that feel "right" without being able to say exactly why — a quality that reads as institutional authority.

#### Relevant to Second Chair
Mallory is a candidate for Second Chair's body copy or UI sans — it carries humanist warmth that makes legal text approachable without becoming casual. The Frere-Jones philosophy that "style is a byproduct of function, not a goal" is directly applicable: Second Chair should never look like it's trying to be stylish; it should look like it's optimized for clarity.

### 3. HOEFLER&CO — typography.com
**New York. Est. 1989. Designers: Jonathan Hoefler.**
**Principal Typefaces: Chronicle, Knockout, Landmark, Whitney, Gotham, Ringside**

#### Site Visual Language
Hoefler&Co's site is the gold standard of a type foundry positioning itself as institutional infrastructure. Clean, bright, editorial — demonstrations of type in use that look like magazine layouts or institutional documents. The site feels like it belongs in a museum gift shop: beautiful, authoritative, expensive.

#### The Design Philosophy
Hoefler&Co has the most prestigious client list in type: The New York Times (Chronicle), Bloomberg Businessweek, The Atlantic, Sports Illustrated. Whitney was used across the Obama administration's government communications. Gotham, commissioned by GQ, became the dominant face of American institutional aspiration in the 2000s.

**Active typefaces of note:**
- **Chronicle Display/Text** — The editorial serif. High-contrast, sharp, unmistakably serious. The face of American prestige publishing. Strong enough to command large headlines; refined enough for long-form text.
- **Knockout** — Condensed grotesque series in dozens of widths. The institutional workhorse sans. Used by anyone who needs compact, authoritative, legible type in confined spaces.
- **Landmark** — A contemporary interpretation of American vernacular lettering — rooted in history, optimized for modern brand applications.
- **Whitney** — Humanist sans designed for institutional environments requiring clarity without coldness. Government, hospitals, wayfinding.
- **Ringside** — Condensed grotesque with sporting and institutional applications. High authority, strong presence.

#### Register
Prestige institutional. The vocabulary of American excellence: NYT, Bloomberg, Obama, The Atlantic. If you use Hoefler fonts, you're borrowing from the most trusted institutions in American cultural life.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
**Yes — and at the highest level.** Hoefler's Chronicle is simultaneously a 21st-century design and unmistakably continuous with the tradition of transitional serifs going back to Baskerville. It earns its authority through historical continuity, not by declaring itself modern.

#### Relevant to Second Chair
Chronicle Display is worth serious consideration for Second Chair's headline face — it is the typeface that says "this organization handles important things seriously." The Obama administration chose Whitney for government communications because it communicated exactly: authoritative, trustworthy, clear, human. That exact register is what Second Chair needs with PI attorneys.

### 4. COMMERCIAL TYPE — commercialtype.com
**New York/London. Est. 2004. Designers: Paul Barnes, Christian Schwartz, and partners.**
**Principal Typefaces: Guardian Egyptian, Publico, Canela, Graphik, Styrene**

#### Site Visual Language
Commercial Type's site does something unusual: it presents the foundry as both type studio and design studio. The opening statement — "Commercial Type creates new typefaces with firm roots in history. We specialize equally in commissioned work for clients and an extensive library for general use." — is a mission statement that could apply to Second Chair's own creative positioning. The site demonstrates editorial gravitas through its custom work list: Rolling Stone, OMNY (NYC transit), V&A, Chobani.

#### Color System
The site presents type specimens in large format against white — specimens are the visual content. Clean structure, hierarchical navigation, editorial in feeling.

#### The Design Philosophy
Commercial Type's core thesis: typefaces with historical roots, executed with contemporary production quality, for editorial and institutional contexts. This is the "new classic" position stated plainly.

**Active typefaces of note:**
- **Canela** — "A graceful display typeface that defies traditional classifications." Begun as a Caslon interpretation, evolved into something between sans and serif — "vestigial flaring at the ends of strokes" creating a "monumental quality influenced by stone carving." First appeared in *Document Journal* (fashion/art magazine). Carries "sober elegance" and "quiet confidence." Canela is one of the most important typefaces of the 2020s — it threads the needle between classical and contemporary so completely that it refuses to be categorized.
- **Guardian Egyptian** — Commissioned by The Guardian newspaper, designed for maximum legibility at every size. Now the standard for editorial slab serif.
- **Publico** — Text serif designed for demanding print conditions. Portuguese newspaper heritage. Dense, readable, authoritative.
- **Graphik** — Neutral grotesque with warmth. The Commercial Type answer to Helvetica: geometric without being cold, neutral without being invisible.
- **Styrene** — Contemporary grotesque with mechanical tension. Used in industrial and institutional contexts.

#### Custom Work Clients
- Rolling Stone (new type direction)
- OMNY — NYC MTA transit system titling
- l'eggs (logotype evolution)
- Chobani Serif (custom brand serif for Chobani)
- V&A Museum (London)

This client list is instructive: consumer brands that want institutional authority (Chobani), cultural institutions that want contemporary relevance (V&A), and infrastructure projects requiring absolute legibility at scale (NYC transit).

#### Register
Editorial. Cultural. Institutional. The vocabulary of ambitious organizations that take design seriously as a business decision.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
**Yes — most explicitly.** The mission statement says it: "firm roots in history." Canela is the clearest embodiment: it looks both ancient (stone carving heritage) and utterly contemporary (precise digital construction, used in fashion/art publishing).

#### Relevant to Second Chair
Canela is a compelling Second Chair headline candidate — it carries the "monumental" quality that would distinguish a PI legal brand from every billboard competitor, while the "sober elegance" prevents it from reading as pretentious. Graphik or Styrene as body sans would create a pairing that is simultaneously editorial and legible. The Chobani precedent is particularly instructive: Chobani used Commercial Type to transform from commodity yogurt brand to premium lifestyle company. Second Chair is making a parallel move — from commodity lead vendor to premium attorney partnership.

### 8. HARVARD LAW REVIEW — harvardlawreview.org
**The Upstatement Redesign — The Gold Standard for Legal Institutional Digital Design**

#### Typeface
The Harvard Law Review site uses a serif headline face (consistent with their editorial identity — likely a version of a Georgian or institutional serif) with a clean, restrained sans for navigation, tags, and UI elements. The hierarchy is: Topic Category (sans, small caps or uppercase) → Article Title (serif, large, authoritative) → Author name (sans, smaller weight). This mirrors the structure of the print journal itself.

#### Color
The Harvard Law Review color system draws from the institution: deep crimson/garnet as the primary accent, deployed sparingly. Against white ground and near-black text, the crimson appears only where it needs to declare authority — links, key UI elements, section markers. The restraint makes the crimson feel earned rather than decorative.

#### Layout Principles
The layout is severely disciplined:
- Strict typographic hierarchy: category tag → article title → author → excerpt
- No illustrations, no hero photography
- Articles are presented as a scholarly journal would present them — title and author are sufficient; the content speaks for itself
- Generous margins, readable line lengths, careful leading
- The "Recently Cited" section (court opinions and scholarship that have cited HLR) is the most important status signal on the page — and it's presented with no fanfare, just clean type

#### What the Design Communicates
Every design decision says: *this content is serious enough that it needs no decoration.* The design's restraint is itself the authority claim. No law firm website looks like this — and that's exactly why it's the model. Attorneys who see this design register it immediately as belonging to a different category than anything they've seen from a legal service provider.

#### Content Architecture Translated
The HLR's content structure — Articles, Essays, Notes, Book Reviews, Tributes, Forum, Blog — is a model for how Second Chair could structure its own thought leadership: not a "Resources" page, but a clearly differentiated editorial hierarchy that signals depth and rigor.

#### Register
Scholarly. Institutional. Authoritative. The design of earned permanence — this is not a brand trying to seem important; it is a 137-year-old institution that knows it is.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
**Yes — the Upstatement redesign specifically.** Upstatement (the Boston design firm) took a legacy institution and gave it digital clarity without stripping its authority. The result is simultaneously contemporary in its execution (clean, responsive, whitespace-generous) and continuous with 137 years of institutional identity.

#### Relevant to Second Chair
This is the most direct design model for Second Chair's institutional positioning. The specific elements to translate:
- **Topic/category tags** as small uppercase sans labels before article titles → Second Chair equivalent: service type or client firm tier labels
- **Article title as primary hierarchy** — large, serif, no need for a subhead → Second Chair equivalent: case study titles, insight titles
- **Author byline structure** → Second Chair equivalent: attorney partner names, firm names as the "author" of a case outcome
- **"Recently cited" as the status signal** → Second Chair equivalent: case results, settlement figures presented as clean typographic data
- **No photography on editorial pages** → When Second Chair presents thought leadership, type carries the authority; photography belongs only in testimonial and case contexts

### 9. PENGUIN RANDOM HOUSE — penguinrandomhouse.com
**The Book Publishing Model — Editorial Hierarchy at Consumer Scale**

#### Current Design Assessment
The Penguin Random House *parent company* site has evolved toward mass-market commercial functionality — it is effectively a book retail destination rather than an editorial statement. The book-cover-as-hero format, the recommendations engine, the genre navigation — these are all conversion-optimized retail patterns, not editorial publishing.

**However:** The Penguin *imprint* level — specifically Penguin Classics and Penguin Modern Classics — remains one of the strongest living traditions of editorial design. Penguin Classics' power is that the design has not changed since 1935 — the orange stripe, the clean sans title, the author name in a different weight, no illustration except for cover art. The design is so consistent that it has become the signal of "serious canonical literature." When you see a Penguin Classic spine on a shelf, you know exactly what it means before you read the title.

**The brand lesson for Second Chair:** Consistency at the system level creates the same recognition effect that a Penguin Classic spine creates. You don't need to declare your authority in every touchpoint — you need a system consistent enough that the system itself becomes the authority signal.

#### Register
The Penguin Classics register: canonical, permanent, serious, accessible. Not intimidating. Not exclusive. But unmistakably in a different category than the mass-market.

#### Classic-Modern Synthesis?
At the imprint level: completely. Penguin Classics is perhaps the oldest continuous example of the synthesis — unchanged because the synthesis was right the first time.

#### Relevant to Second Chair
**The Penguin Classics orange spine model** — a visual system so consistent that it becomes instantly recognizable — is the long-term aspiration for Second Chair. Not individual beautiful moments, but a system of such integrity that attorney-partners recognize Second Chair documents, emails, case files, and presentations as belonging to the same entity at a glance, without needing to see a logo.

# The New Classic Movement in Design (2022–2026)

*What is happening in design right now. Why it matters for Second Chair. And what to do with it.*

---

## What Is It?

The New Classic Movement is not a named trend. It has no manifesto. It has no founders. It emerged as a reaction and a revival simultaneously — and it is the dominant aesthetic position of the most sophisticated practitioners working in 2022-2026.

**The defining characteristics:**

1. **Strong editorial serif as the headline face** — not a delicate old-style serif but a confident, high-contrast, optically precise serif with historical roots and contemporary production quality. Tiempos. Chronicle. Canela. GT Sectra. Editorial New. Signifier. Ogg.

2. **Refined grotesque or humanist sans as the body/UI face** — not Inter, not DM Sans, not Circular. These are the grotesques with optical depth: Neue Haas Grotesk, Söhne, Founders Grotesk, Graphik, Suisse Int'l. Faces that have ancestry and craft visible in their construction.

3. **White space as intention, not absence** — generous margins and leading that signal confidence. "We have nothing to prove; we don't need to fill every pixel." The space is the argument.

4. **Institutional color systems** — warm white (#F7F4EF or similar) or pure white, deep near-black text (#1A1A1A), and one single accent color deployed with maximum restraint. Often: crimson, deep navy, or hunter green. Never: startup blue, gradient, or neon.

5. **Photography as documentation, not decoration** — when images appear, they read as archival or photojournalistic. Real situations, real people, real evidence. Not stock photography of handshakes.

6. **Structure as meaning** — the design of information hierarchy carries the authority claim. Category → Title → Author → Content is not just a layout decision; it is a statement about what kind of organization this is.

---

## What Is It Reacting Against?

To understand the New Classic Movement, you have to understand what preceded it and why it failed aesthetically.

**2014–2022: The Era of SaaS Homogeneity**

The decade of venture-funded startups produced a design language that became so pervasive it became invisible — and then became a symbol of distrust.

The signature elements:
- **Inter or Circular as the default typeface** — clean, neutral, optimized for screen legibility, utterly without personality
- **Blue gradient hero sections** — the visual signal for "this is a tech product"
- **Rounded corners everywhere** — softened edges as a proxy for friendliness
- **Abstract illustration** — isometric icons of people doing activities, geometric shapes, "diversity" clip-art updated for the 2010s
- **Split-screen hero layouts** — headline left, product screenshot right
- **Conversion optimization as the only design goal** — every decision subordinated to the click

This design language was built for one purpose: to get venture-backed B2B companies past the visual bar of "looks legit enough to sign up." It succeeded at that goal — and in succeeding, it became generic. By 2020, every piece of software, every SaaS product, every DTC brand, every fintech startup looked identical.

**The Backlash: Why the New Classic Emerged**

Three forces converged to make the New Classic the dominant counter-position by 2022:

**1. Trust exhaustion with digital products.** Consumers and B2B buyers grew skeptical of brands that "look like an app." The SaaS aesthetic became associated not with quality but with interchangeability. When everything looks the same, looking different is the first differentiator.

**2. The prestige migration.** The most admired brands of the 2020s — Chobani (Commercial Type), Stripe (Chronicle + restrained editorial), The New York Times (Chronicle + whitespace), The Atlantic (Chronicle + its editorial depth) — had quietly adopted the vocabulary of institutional design. Ambitious brands noticed. The Chobani rebrand is the case study: commodity yogurt brand → premium lifestyle company through typography alone.

**3. The AI visual content crisis.** As AI-generated imagery became both ubiquitous and recognizable, photographic image-making was devalued as a differentiator. The response from serious designers: double down on typography, which AI cannot yet replicate at the craft level of a Klim or a Hoefler. Typographic sophistication became the signal of human expertise and genuine design investment.

---

## What Is the Shared Aesthetic Principle?

Across every foundry, publication, and institution documented in this research, there is one shared principle:

**Historical authority + contemporary precision = institutional permanence.**

- Klim makes better versions of Plantin, Akzidenz, Helvetica, Futura, Franklin Gothic. Historical authority, contemporary precision.
- Hoefler&Co's Chronicle is unmistakably continuous with transitional serif history — and unmistakably a 21st-century design.
- Commercial Type's Canela roots itself in Caslon and stone carving — and debutes in a fashion/art magazine in 2020.
- The Harvard Law Review carries 137 years of institutional authority into a contemporary digital design executed by Upstatement.
- Penguin Classics has not changed its core design since 1935 — and is more recognizable now than any brand that redesigns every five years.

The principle is not "look old." The principle is: **earn permanence through rootedness, not novelty.**

Brands that look like they arrived last year will look dated next year. Brands that look like they have always existed and will always exist occupy a different category — they appear to have achieved the timelessness that can only come from being genuinely good.

---

## Why It Matters for Second Chair Specifically

Second Chair operates in a category defined by visual chaos.

PI attorney advertising is the loudest, most garish, most visually aggressive category in American marketing. Billboards with screaming attorneys. TV commercials with 1-800 numbers in giant type. Legal websites with dark backgrounds, stock photographs of crashed cars, and CTAs in aggressive red. The visual vocabulary of PI lead generation communicates urgency through volume — everything is turned up to maximum because no one has dared turn it down.

**Second Chair's opportunity is precisely this: be the first entity in PI legal lead generation that looks like it doesn't need to scream.**

The New Classic Movement provides the aesthetic vocabulary for that positioning:

**What "I don't need to scream" looks like:**
- Headlines in Tiempos or Chronicle at a considered size, not enormous
- White space that says "we have nothing to prove"
- Data presented as clean type, not charts exploding with color
- Attorney partner names set with the same dignity as a law review author byline
- Case results displayed like financial figures — quietly, precisely, without exclamation points

**The specific translation:**

| Design Element | Generic PI Lead Gen | Second Chair New Classic |
|---|---|---|
| Headline face | Arial Bold / Impact / a rounded sans | Tiempos Headline or Chronicle Display |
| Body text | Generic sans (Inter, DM Sans) | Söhne or Neue Haas Grotesk |
| Background | Dark/navy with bright accents | Warm white (#F7F4EF) or white |
| Accent color | Aggressive red or electric blue | Deep navy (#1A2540) or measured crimson (#8B1A1A) |
| Data display | Bar charts, infographics | Clean typographic tables — the numbers speak |
| Photography | Crashed cars, gavels, stock handshakes | Attorneys at work, documents, the details of real practice |
| CTA buttons | Bright red/green, rounded, aggressive | Understated, contained, high-contrast — confidence not urgency |
| Content structure | Resources / FAQ / Testimonials | Articles / Case Studies / Insights / Results |

## What the New Classic Is NOT

Understanding what not to do is as important as understanding what to do.

**The New Classic is not:**
- Stuffy. Historical rootedness does not mean stiff. Chronicle is serious and alive. Tiempos is warm. Canela has "quiet confidence" — not stiffness.
- A museum. These typefaces are used by *Document Journal* (fashion), Rolling Stone (music), Chobani (yogurt). The aesthetic is not confined to academic publishing.
- Inaccessible. The New Classic is readable. The whole point of editorial typography is maximum comprehension. It excludes no one — it includes everyone who takes reading seriously.
- Expensive-looking in the decorative sense. The aesthetic communicates quality through restraint, not through gold letterheads or drop shadows.
- Retro. This is not 1950s nostalgia. Canela is between ancient stone carving and 2020 fashion editorial — simultaneously ancient and absolutely contemporary.

## The Actionable Takeaway for Second Chair

Second Chair should position itself visually as the *first company in PI legal lead generation that takes its own design as seriously as a law firm, a law review, or a prestige publisher.*

The competitive differentiation is immediate and clear: every other company in this space looks like it came from the same template. Second Chair would look like it came from a different category entirely.

The design vocabulary to study:
1. **Type:** Tiempos Headline (Klim) or Chronicle Display (Hoefler&Co) for headlines. Söhne (Klim) or Neue Haas Grotesk for body/UI.
2. **Color:** Warm white ground + near-black text + ONE measured accent (deep navy or institutional crimson) deployed with maximum restraint.
3. **Space:** Generous. Confident. The silence is intentional.
4. **Content structure:** Editorial hierarchy. Not a product page — a publication.
5. **Photography:** Photojournalistic register. The documents and details of real practice.
6. **Data:** Typographic. Numbers in a well-set table communicate more authority than any infographic.

The attorney partners Second Chair wants are the same attorneys who went to law schools that have Harvard Law Review on their tables. They know what authority looks like. Second Chair's design should speak their language — not the language of the billboard across from the courthouse.

## Reference Typeface Shortlist for Second Chair Design System

### Headline / Display (the authority voice)
| Typeface | Foundry | Character | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tiempos Headline** | Klim | Warm + sharp. Editorial authority without stuffiness. Institutional but alive. | Primary recommendation — balances dignity and warmth |
| **Chronicle Display** | Hoefler&Co | High contrast, prestige American publishing. NYT-level authority. | Maximum institutional weight |
| **Canela** | Commercial Type | Between ancient and contemporary. Stone carving + fashion editorial. | Where Second Chair wants a more distinctive, less conventional voice |
| **GT Sectra** | Grilli Type | Nordic editorial precision. Sharp, rigorous, confident. | If Second Chair leans into the analytical/data dimension |

### Body / UI (the clarity voice)
| Typeface | Foundry | Character | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Söhne** | Klim | Refined Akzidenz. Warm but disciplined. The best grotesque for institutions that don't want to be cold. | Primary recommendation |
| **Neue Haas Grotesk** | Linotype | The original Helvetica, fully optically corrected. Neutral authority. | Maximum neutrality at small sizes |
| **Founders Grotesk** | Klim | American newspaper grotesque heritage. Workmanlike authority. | If Second Chair wants more American vernacular weight |
| **Graphik** | Commercial Type | Contemporary neutral grotesque. Warm enough for long form, precise enough for UI. | If Canela is the headline, Graphik pairs naturally |

### Pairing Recommendation
**Primary:** Tiempos Headline + Söhne. The editorial serif + refined grotesque. The Klim house pairing used by Cambridge Audio, institutions, and ambitious brands worldwide.

**Alternative:** Chronicle Display + Neue Haas Grotesk. The prestige American pairing. What the NYT and Bloomberg use.

**Distinctive:** Canela + Graphik. Commercial Type's house pairing. More idiosyncratic — for when Second Chair wants to be recognized by designers as well as attorneys.
