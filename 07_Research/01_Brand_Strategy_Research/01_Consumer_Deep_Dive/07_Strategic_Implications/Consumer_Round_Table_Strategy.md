## Question 1: Primary Audience — B2B Attorney or B2C Claimant?

**Julian Cole:**
"The business starts with the B2B relationship. Second Chair sells to attorneys. That's the revenue model. But the consumer research shows that B2C advertising quality directly affects B2B satisfaction and retention. So: which audience is primary, strategically?"

**Byron Sharp:**
"From a mental availability standpoint, you can't ignore either. But the question of primary audience is really a question of what Second Chair builds first. You need the B2B product to work before B2C advertising has somewhere to send claimants."

**Richard Shotton:**
"I'd argue the sequencing is: build B2B first, but build it *with* B2C in mind from day one. The attorney purchase decision is partly about trusting that Second Chair's advertising is honest. You can't make that claim without having honest advertising to show. B2C advertising is the evidence for the B2B claim."

**Jon Steel:**
"The two audiences are in service to each other. The attorney who fights for the little guy needs clients who came in honestly. The accident victim who needs someone in their corner needs an attorney who takes their ethics seriously enough to care about where leads come from. These two consumers find each other through Second Chair. That's the brand story."

**Les Binet:**
"Commercially, B2B is primary. That's where the revenue lives. But strategically, B2C advertising is what makes the B2B brand distinctively defensible. No other vendor can replicate 'honest advertising' as a B2B claim without actually running honest advertising."

**Nigel Bogle:**
"We can't have the B2C strategy without the B2B product functioning. I'd set B2B as the operational priority and B2C as the brand priority. The brand is built through B2C. The business runs on B2B."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **B2B is the operational and revenue priority. B2C advertising is the strategic differentiator that makes the B2B brand defensible and distinctive. Second Chair builds the B2B product first; the B2C advertising strategy is developed in parallel and launched as proof of the B2B brand promise, not independently.**

## Question 2: Primary B2B Consumer — Which Attorney Profile?

**Julian Cole:**
"The profile research gives us four buyers: Tony Reyes (hungry solo), Jennifer Walsh (established builder), Marcus Chen (marketing director), Bill Taggart (tired king). Who is Second Chair's primary consumer and what does that mean for strategy?"

**Richard Roma:**
"Tony Reyes and Jennifer Walsh. The solo and the small firm managing partner. They're the highest volume, they're the most emotionally responsive to the certainty message, and they're the most likely to be reached through peer channels. Marcus Chen is a great long-term client but the sales cycle is longer and his firm has multiple vendors — he's not going to move fast."

**Byron Sharp:**
"Agree on Tony and Jennifer as volume targets, but the CEP analysis suggests they operate on very different timescales. Tony enters the buying category in short, acute windows — the slow month, the bad vendor experience. Jennifer enters in longer, more strategic windows — post-trial recovery, firm expansion. Different CEP timing means different media strategy for the same 'primary audience.'"

**Adam Ferrier:**
"The behavior change brief is very different for Tony vs. Jennifer. Tony needs motivation reduction (lower the risk of trying) and ability reduction (make switching trivially easy). Jennifer needs motivation addition (I can get to $5M faster) and ability addition (volume and reporting she can justify internally). Same sales story, very different behavioral interventions."

**Richard Shotton:**
"Don't underestimate Bill Taggart as a strategic account. He's harder to reach but his account LTV is significantly higher, and his peer network is the most influential. One Bill Taggart who becomes an advocate is worth 20 Tony Reyes as a referral source."

**Nigel Bogle:**
"From a commercial architecture perspective: Tony Reyes is the volume base, Jennifer Walsh is the growth tier, Marcus Chen is the sophistication tier, Bill Taggart is the prestige tier. Build the product and go-to-market for Jennifer (the growth tier) and you'll serve Tony well, build toward Marcus, and set up Bill."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **Primary B2B consumer: Jennifer Walsh (established builder, 3-10 attorneys). She is the growth tier — sophisticated enough to track ROI, hungry enough to prioritize marketing, accessible enough to reach through direct sales. Tony Reyes (hungry solo) is the accessible volume at entry; Marcus Chen is the long-term sophistication tier. Bill Taggart is strategic account territory, reached through peer referral not direct sales. Product and messaging should be built for Jennifer and will stretch appropriately to Tony and Marcus.**

## Question 3: The B2C Consumer — Which Moment Deserves Most Investment?

**Julian Cole:**
"CONSUMER_CEP_MAP.md identifies five claimant CEPs. We can't invest equally in all of them. Where should the majority of B2C advertising investment go?"

**Adam Ferrier:**
"The 72-hour window and the settlement offer moment. Those are the two moments where the behavior change is still possible. All other moments are either too early (pre-accident brand awareness) or too late (post-signature regret). The 72-hour window is the hardest to reach; the settlement offer moment is the clearest decision trigger."

**Byron Sharp:**
"Pre-accident priming is undervalued by Ferrier's analysis. The mental availability built before the accident is what makes 'one call before you decide' feel familiar when the accident happens. The victim who has heard the message before doesn't have to process it from scratch in their 72-hour cognitive overload state."

**Jon Steel:**
"The social referral moment is strategically distinct from the others. You can't buy it. You can only enable it. If the advertising creates vocabulary that people can share — 'before you sign anything, call first' — the social referral moment happens organically and carries more trust than any paid media."

**Richard Shotton:**
"The settlement offer moment is the highest behavioral leverage point. It's the last moment before the irreversible decision. Loss framing at this moment is the most efficient behavioral intervention available. I'd weight investment heavily here."

**Les Binet:**
"The effectiveness evidence supports a split: 60% of B2C budget to pre-accident brand building (creates the cognitive scaffold that activates at the CEP), 40% to timing-targeted performance advertising at the 72-hour window and settlement offer moment. The brand building is slow but compounding; the performance advertising is immediate but doesn't compound."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **B2C advertising investment split: 60% to pre-accident brand awareness building ("one call before you decide" instruction in the general population); 40% to timing-targeted performance advertising at the 72-hour window and settlement offer CEPs. Social referral moment is enabled through advertising but not purchased — create the vocabulary, trust the organic spread. The 60/40 split follows Binet/Field effectiveness evidence and builds the compounding brand layer that performance advertising alone cannot create.**

## Question 4: The Identity Barrier — How Do We Solve "I'm Not the Kind of Person Who Sues"?

**Julian Cole:**
"The single largest motivation barrier for accident victims is identity-based. The research is clear on this. What's the strategic solution?"

**Adam Ferrier:**
"Identity reframing. Never ask the victim to identify as a litigant. Always ask them to identify as an informed decision-maker. The copy instruction: 'find out what you have before you decide' creates a role (information-gatherer) that doesn't conflict with 'I'm not the kind of person who sues.'"

**Richard Shotton:**
"Normalization is the faster route. If the social proof shows that people like them — ordinary, working-class, not litigious — make this call routinely, the identity barrier weakens. The victim who believes 'most people in my situation call first' is less likely to see themselves as abnormal for doing so."

**Jon Steel:**
"The identity reframe has to be inside the character story, not just in the copy. The testimonial character who 'almost didn't call' because she 'doesn't want to be that person' — but decided that finding out what she had was different from suing — does the identity reframe through story, which is more durable than copy doing it through argument."

**John Hegarty** *(contributing note from creative analysis):*
"Make the identity of 'person who makes informed decisions' aspirational. Not 'it's okay to call' (permission-based, slightly defensive) but 'the smart thing to do before any major financial decision is to get the information first' (identity-aspirational). The former tells them it's acceptable; the latter tells them it's smart."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **Three-layer identity solution: (1) Reframe the action as "informed decision-making," not "claiming" or "suing" — in all copy, in all executions; (2) Normalization through ordinary-person social proof showing that "people like you" make this call routinely; (3) Aspirational framing that makes information-gathering before a major decision feel smart and self-respecting, not aggressive. The word "sue" does not appear in Second Chair B2C advertising. The word "call" replaces it at every instance.**

## Question 5: B2B — What Is the Most Defensible Single Claim?

**Julian Cole:**
"Every other vendor in the category claims quality, exclusivity, and conversion. What is the single most defensible claim Second Chair can make that no other vendor can replicate?"

**Richard Shotton:**
"'We'll show you the advertising before you see the lead.' This is verifiable, specific, and structurally exclusive — it requires the vendor to actually be running honest advertising to make it. Anyone can claim transparency. Second Chair is the only vendor who can demonstrate it."

**Byron Sharp:**
"Agree. The 'show the advertising' claim creates a distinctive mental hook that also serves as a proof point for every other claim. If they show the advertising, and the advertising is honest, the claim that leads come from honest advertising becomes verifiable rather than asserted."

**Jon Steel:**
"The claim that runs under 'show the advertising' is: 'Second Chair is the only lead gen company whose advertising you'd be comfortable showing your clients.' This is the identity version of the transparency claim. It speaks to what the attorney cares about most deeply — not just the conversion rate but the professional integrity of how they got the client."

**Les Binet:**
"From an effectiveness standpoint, 'show the advertising' is a brand claim that builds over time. Early adopters who experience it tell peers. Peers tell other peers. The claim becomes category-defining. Five years from now, attorneys will ask every vendor to show them the advertising — because Second Chair established that standard."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **Second Chair's single most defensible B2B claim: 'We'll show you our advertising before you commit to anything.' This is the core proof of the Second Chair brand promise, delivered as an invitation rather than an assertion. It is structurally exclusive (requires actually running honest advertising), verifiable (you can see it), and addresses the deepest attorney concern (advertising ethics). All other B2B messaging — exclusivity, quality, conversion, compliance — flows from this central claim as supporting evidence.**

## Question 6: The Richard Roma Finding — January Is the Sales Window

**Julian Cole:**
"Roma's analysis establishes January-March as the peak B2B buying window. What are the strategic implications?"

**Richard Roma:**
"The sales team should be rested and fully resourced in January. Q4 is relationship-building and content seeding. Q1 is when you pick up the phone and make the calls. The seasonal pattern is real — the attorney's anxiety peaks in winter, their openness to new vendors peaks in winter. If you're not selling in January, you're giving the window to competitors."

**Adam Ferrier:**
"The motivation is naturally high in January — the attorney is anxious about their pipeline. That means the ability barrier is the primary constraint. Lower the ability barrier as much as possible in January: simplest possible pilot offer, no contract, easiest possible setup. The simpler the entry, the higher the conversion."

**Nigel Bogle:**
"The Q1 sales window argument supports a different budget allocation than most lead gen companies use. They spend when accident volume is highest (summer). Second Chair should spend when attorney buying intent is highest (winter). Counter-cyclical investment creates the advantage."

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **January-March is Second Chair's primary B2B sales window. Q4 investment in brand and relationship-building sets up Q1 outreach. The Q1 B2B advertising and sales strategy should be maximally friction-free: simplest pilot offer, most aggressive reference program, most available sales team. Counter-cyclical investment in winter (when accident volume is low but attorney anxiety is high) is the differentiated timing strategy that delivers superior ROI.**

"Second Chair is the vendor that answers the question every attorney has been asking: 'Can I see what you're running on my behalf?' Yes. Here it is."

**Julian Cole:**
"They're all right. Here's the synthesis: **Second Chair is certainty for the attorney and honesty for the claimant, delivered by the same act: advertising that doesn't lie.**"

**DECISION — Julian Cole:**
> **Second Chair's strategic identity: The PI lead gen company whose product is honest advertising — which creates certainty for attorneys (reliable pipeline, no ethics risk) and honesty for claimants (accurate information before a major decision). Both audiences served by the same core act. This is not a positioning claim. It is an operational commitment that generates a brand. Everything Second Chair does — product, sales, marketing, creative — flows from the decision to advertise honestly.**
