## What This Session Must Answer

The 4Cs framework defines Culture as: **tensions, movements, and purpose opportunities.** Three questions drive the entire session:

1. What's going on in culture that Second Chair could credibly **challenge**?
2. What, in culture, could Second Chair become a credible **champion** of?
3. What **noble purpose** could Second Chair uniquely and credibly pursue?

Because Second Chair operates on two fronts simultaneously — selling to PI attorneys (B2B) and running consumer ads that reach accident victims (B2C) — these three questions must be answered **twice**, for two culturally distinct audiences whose worlds intersect but do not overlap.

---

## The Structural Difference from Sessions 01 & 02

Sessions 01 (Consumer) and 02 (Category) were strategy-team only. Culture is where the brand's emotional purpose lives, which means the **creative team enters the research for the first time**:

- **John Hegarty (ECD):** What cultural territory is brave enough to build a brand around? His lens is the "zag" — the cultural white space that produces fame.
- **Ed McCabe (Copywriter):** What cultural tensions produce the sharpest language? What is the verbal articulation of the cultural position?
- **Graham Fink (Art Director):** What does this cultural territory look like? How does culture inform visual execution?

Strategy identifies what's true in culture. Creative identifies what's **usable** in culture. The round table where both teams collide produces the inputs that feed directly into the Creative Brief.

## Phase 0: Research Agenda Mapping Results

Before any research was conducted, Julian Cole mapped the specific evidence each agent needs in order to complete their analysis without gaps. These needs were built into the research workstreams upfront, preventing the mid-analysis research loops that extended the Category session.

| Agent | What They Need From Culture Research |
|-------|-------------------------------------|
| **Byron Sharp** | Which cultural disruption moments create CEP-like openings (when attorneys' minds are open to new vendors). Examples of brands that entered a category during cultural disruption and built mental availability quickly. Which cultural phenomena create durable memory structures vs. PR spikes. |
| **Richard Shotton** | Documented behavioral norms in PI attorney culture — what attorneys do by default. How the "ambulance chaser" narrative has become internalized behavioral inertia. How norm violation (honest in a dishonest industry) has historically generated attention in professional cultures. |
| **Les Binet & Peter Field** | IPA case studies of purpose-led brands in professional services and B2B. The effectiveness track record of "honest in a dishonest industry" positioning. How cultural purpose translates to "fame" and commercial results. |
| **Adam Ferrier** | The specific cultural barriers preventing accident victims from seeking legal help. What cultural permission a claimant needs before they will respond to honest legal advertising. Friction embedded in cultural norms (minimization instinct, "not the kind of person who sues"). |
| **Jon Steel** | The emotionally resonant stories inside both cultural tensions — not as concepts but as lived experiences with language. The specific words PI attorneys use when talking about pride, shame, and their mission among themselves. The claimant's internal narrative about seeking legal help. |
| **John Hegarty** | What cultural territory has NOT been claimed in PI legal advertising. Examples of brands that made the "honest" position creatively interesting, not earnest or boring. Where is the cultural territory brave enough to build a brand around? |
| **Ed McCabe** | The actual language: what PI attorneys say privately vs. publicly. What phrases carry the real weight of each cultural tension. The vocabulary of accident victims — how they describe their situation, fears, and sense of fairness. |
| **Graham Fink** | The visual codes that live inside both cultural worlds — what "trust" looks like visually in PI attorney culture and claimant culture. The visual conventions of PI legal advertising and what the zag looks like. Visual language of "honest in a dishonest industry" brands (Patagonia, Oatly, Basecamp). |
