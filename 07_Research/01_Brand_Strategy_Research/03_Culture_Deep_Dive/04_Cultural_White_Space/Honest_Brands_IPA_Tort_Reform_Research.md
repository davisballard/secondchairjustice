# TOPIC 1: "Honest in a Dishonest Industry" Brand Cases

## 1.1 Patagonia

### "Don't Buy This Jacket" Campaign (November 2011)

- **Placement:** Full-page advertisement in *The New York Times* on Black Friday
- **Headline:** "Don't Buy This Jacket"
- **Product:** R2 jacket (environmental cost breakdown: 135 liters of water, 20 lbs CO2, two-thirds of jacket weight in waste)
- **Business results:** Patagonia's revenue surged **30% the following year** (Marketing Week, Ainoa Agency, Syncblogs)
- **Rationale (from Patagonia):** "It's time for us as a company to address the issue of consumerism and do it head on... The most challenging, and important, element of the Common Threads Initiative is this: to lighten our environmental footprint, everyone needs to consume less."
- **Chouinard on hypocrisy:** "It would be hypocritical for us to work for environmental change without encouraging customers to think before they buy."
- **Source:** Patagonia Stories, Science of Retail, Marketing Week

### 1% for the Planet & Corporate Structure Change (September 2022)

- **Announcement date:** September 14, 2022
- **Yvon Chouinard quote:** "Instead of extracting value from nature and transforming it into wealth, we are using the wealth Patagonia creates to protect the source. **We're making Earth our only shareholder.** I am dead serious about saving this planet."
- **Structure:** 
  - **Patagonia Purpose Trust** — 2% voting stock, ensures founder's values persist
  - **Holdfast Collective** — 98% nonvoting stock, nonprofit receiving ~$100M annually for environmental causes
- **Company valuation:** ~$3 billion
- **Chouinard on alternatives:** Rejected selling or going public; described move as "going purpose" rather than "going public"
- **Sources:** Patagonia Works press, Business of Fashion, HBR, Fast Company, CNBC, Washington Post

### Cultural Territory

- **Position:** "The honest outdoor brand" — sustainability as genuine mission, not marketing
- **Credibility built through:** Common Threads Initiative (reduce, repair, reuse, recycle), Worn Wear used gear program, B Corp status, 1% of sales to grassroots activists
- **Counterintuitive strategy:** Telling customers *not* to buy on Black Friday created stopping power; authenticity + values alignment drove loyalty

## 1.2 Oatly

### Ingredient Transparency & Anti-Dairy Positioning

- **Agency:** Forsman & Bodenfors (Swedish)
- **"Wow, No Cow" campaign (2014):** Oatly CEO Toni Petersson performed a song in a film explaining the brand mission; later expanded to Super Bowl 2021 (subsequently banned in Sweden after dairy lobby lawsuit)
- **Packaging as main media:** Oatly treated packaging as primary ad channel — irreverent copywriting, unpredictable designs functioning like posters
- **Custom typefaces:** Toni Grotesk and John Rounded (designed by Lars Elfman, named after founder Toni Petersson and creative director John Schoolcraft)
- **Reframe:** Shifted from "allergy product" to "lifestyle choice" — for people who *don't want* cow's milk, not just those who can't have it
- **Result:** Won Silver Lion at Cannes 2015 for Brand Identity Experience
- **Sources:** Fonts in Use, Forsman & Bodenfors, AdsSpot, The Drum, Medium

### Climate Footprint Challenge (May 2023)

- **Tactic:** Two-page print ads in NYT, LA Times, Washington Post; billboards in Times Square and Hollywood
- **Page 1:** Oatly's own climate footprint statistics
- **Page 2:** Reserved for dairy companies willing to disclose climate impact — **free ad space** as incentive
- **Stat cited:** 144 gallons of water required for one gallon of cow's milk; Oatly Original has ~50% climate impact of comparable cow's milk
- **"Brainwashing" framing:** Oatly calls its advertising "Brainwashing" — provocative, unconventional, designed to intrigue
- **Dairy Deprogramming Zone:** Soft serve truck across from Capitol Hill Ice Cream Party to expose dairy industry's lack of climate transparency
- **Sources:** Marketing Dive, Oatly (Climate Footprint Challenge), Oatly "Brainwashing" pages

### What Made It Work

- **Positioning against industry:** Framed dairy industry as bully when they sued to block messaging — made Oatly look like underdog
- **Minimal media budget:** "Unconventional despite a minimal media budget" (Medium/The Go Branding)
- **Less like corporation, more like real people:** Informal design strategy; packaging felt human

## 1.4 Basecamp / 37signals

### "Calm Company" Positioning

- **Core message:** "The answer isn't more hours, it's less bullshit."
- **Book:** *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* (2018) — frames workplace chaos as "perversions of work" and "side effects of broken models"
- **Earlier works:** *REWORK*, *REMOTE* — established foundational principles
- **DHH quote:** "At Basecamp we're trying to provide an alternative role model – or at least an alternative narrative: Here's a company who didn't do any of those things that you say are required to become a successful startup."
- **Philosophy:** 40-hour workweeks, async communication, fixed 6-week product cycles with 2-week cooldowns, rejection of meetings/standups/always-on anxiety
- **Challenge:** Directly opposes Silicon Valley "move fast and break things"
- **Sources:** Signal v. Noise, Basecamp handbook, Balance the Grind

### Visual Identity (Vivar Design)

- **Typography:** Roboto Mono for clarity and modernity
- **Imagery:** Topographic graphics, human-centric photography — exploration and authenticity
- **Logo:** Badges, crests, overlapping geometric forms symbolizing connection
- **Aesthetic:** Organic natural + geometric elements

## 1.5 BrewDog (2007–2015) — Early Punk Positioning

### What Worked

- **Target demographic:** "Punks" — defiant, independent thinkers underserved by Heineken, Carlsberg
- **Competed on attitude, not metrics:** Bold stencil typography, stripped-back labels, edgy names (e.g., "Hello, My Name is Vladimir" — 2014, protesting Russia's gay propaganda law)
- **Provocative stunts:** 2012 — projected naked founders onto Parliament when Diageo blocked Innkeeper of the Year award
- **Earned media through controversy:** No expensive advertising; famous through conflict with Diageo, China, Russia, UK regulators
- **Market results:** Punk IPA best-selling UK craft beer for 3 consecutive years (2014–2017); 5 of top 10 UK craft beers were BrewDog by 2017; UK beer sales declined 2.5%/year (2014–2021) while BrewDog grew **38.5%/year**
- **Sources:** Punks and Pinstripes, Sood Agency, Marketing Week, BBC, The Drum

### What Went Wrong (2018–2022)

- **2018 Pink IPA "for girls"** — backfired; criticized for tone-deaf gender messaging amid known gender pay gap
- **2017:** Aggressive litigation against small businesses ("Draft Punk," "Lone Wolf") — contradicted challenger image
- **2021 BBC doc:** Nearly 300 former/current employees accused founder James Watt of "toxic culture of fear"
- **2022 conclusion:** "The punk ethos isn't working anymore" — £215M annual sales, 2,000 employees made "plucky rule-breaker" identity unsustainable
- **Sources:** Eater London, The Drum, The Guardian, iNews

# TOPIC 2: IPA Effectiveness Cases — Purpose-Led Brands

## 2.1 Peter Field "Effective on Purpose" (2021)

- **Sample:** 47 purpose-driven cases vs. 333 non-purpose cases from IPA Databank
- **Overall:** Non-purpose averaged 1.6 "very large business effects"; purpose averaged 1.1
- **Strong purpose (57% of cases):** 
  - 2.1 very large business effects (vs. 1.6 non-purpose)
  - 50% very large customer acquisition (vs. 30%)
  - 41% very large market share growth (vs. 26%)
  - 3.0 very large brand effects (trust, commitment, fame, differentiation, image)
- **Weak purpose:** 0.1 business effects, 5% customer acquisition, 0% market share
- **Field quote:** "Blanket criticisms of brand purpose advertising are unjustified" — well-executed purpose is "a great new tool for smart brands with the savvy to know how to use it"
- **Sources:** IPA "Power of Brand Purpose," The Drum, Marketing Week, Campaign Asia

## 2.2 IPA Trust-Building Advertising (1998–2024)

- **Sample:** 812 campaigns from IPA Effectiveness Databank; 103 with very large trust increases (£7.5Bn investment)
- **Finding:** **93%** of campaigns with very large trust increases report at least one very large business effect vs. **66%** across all campaigns — **27 percentage points (41%) more effective**
- **Top trust campaigns:** 65% more very large business effects than average
- **Metric outperformance (trust campaigns vs. average):**
  - Customer acquisition: 39% vs 28%
  - Sales volume: 39% vs 25%
  - Profit gain: 35% vs 24%
  - Market share: 34% vs 26%
  - Customer loyalty: 22% vs 11%
  - Brand quality perceptions: 59% vs 20%
  - Brand loyalty: 56% vs 23%
- **Sources:** IPA "Investing in Trust," DecisionMarketing, WARC, Research Live

# TOPIC 3: Tort Reform as Cultural Battleground 2026

## 3.1 US Chamber Institute for Legal Reform (ILR)

### Current Campaigns (2025–2026)

**Municipality Litigation Report (Sept 2025)**  
- Title: "Municipality Litigation: The Plaintiffs' Lawyer Playbook"
- Documents mass lawsuits by cities/counties against corporations
- Six tactical elements: recruiting municipalities, pressuring defendants for settlements that "enrich plaintiffs' attorneys and any third-party investors"
- Case studies: opioid, climate, PFAS, social media, plastics, vaping, vehicle theft
- **Source:** ILR PDF (instituteforlegalreform.com)

**Lawsuit Abuse Reduction Act (LARA) — Reintroduced Sept 2025**  
- Restore mandatory sanctions for frivolous claims (Federal Rule 11)
- End 21-day safe harbor for withdrawal without consequence
- Enable sanctions including attorneys' fees recovery
- ILR framing: Protects small businesses from meritless suits; frees judicial resources
- **Source:** ILR blog

**Protecting Our Courts from Foreign Manipulation Act of 2025**  
- Requires disclosure of foreign-sourced litigation funding
- Prohibits foreign governments and sovereign wealth funds from investing in U.S. lawsuits
- Passed House Judiciary Committee Nov 2025
- **Source:** ILR press releases

### Messaging Themes

- "Lawsuit abuse"
- "Plaintiffs' lawyer playbook"
- "Third-party investors" / "foreign manipulation"
- Small business protection

## 3.3 State-Level Tort Reform (2025)

### Georgia (April 2025)

- **Bills:** Senate Bills 68 and 69 — most significant overhaul since 2005
- **Anti-anchoring:** Plaintiffs cannot present specific dollar values for noneconomic damages; must be "rationally related" to evidence
- **Phantom damages:** Only actually paid medical expenses; future medical based on insurance payment amounts
- **Bifurcation:** In cases >$150K, trials split into liability, compensatory, punitive phases
- **Litigation funding:** Registration requirements (effective Jan 1, 2026)
- **Result:** 20 auto insurers filed rate decreases since Jan 2025; 14 lowered premiums >1%
- **Sources:** DLA Piper, JD Supra, Alston & Bird

### Florida (HB 837 — 2023)

- Two-year statute of limitations, phantom damage elimination, modified comparative negligence
- **Results:** 17 new insurers entered market; nuclear verdict rank dropped from 2nd to 10th nationally; **30% decrease in insurance litigation**
- **Sources:** Florida tort reform, APCIA

### California

- Focus on wildfire/insurance rather than comprehensive tort reform
- **Polling (PACT):** 83% support tort reform; ~75% blame lawsuit abuse for rising costs
- **Source:** Protecting American Consumers

### Texas

- Texans for Lawsuit Reform active; "trial lawyers must be stopped" framing
- **Source:** tortreform.com

# SPECIALIST EVIDENCE

## For John Hegarty: Cultural Territory & the Zag

### What Has NOT Been Claimed in PI Legal Advertising

- **"Honest" positioning** — no major PI lead gen vendor has claimed "the honest one"
- **Anti-ambulance-chaser** — no one has explicitly positioned *against* the sleaze perception of the category
- **Calm, professional, dignified** — category is high-energy, urgent, sometimes aggressive
- **Transparency about the model** — most vendors hide or obfuscate lead sourcing, shared vs. exclusive, quality metrics

### What Would a "Zag" Look Like?

- **Patagonia/Oatly model:** Admit uncomfortable truths ("We're not the cheapest"; "Some leads won't convert"; "We can't promise every lead is perfect") — earns trust
- **Basecamp model:** "Calm vendor" — no pressure, no hype, no FOMO; clarity over clamor
- **Cards Against Humanity caution:** Radical honesty only works when *genuine* — BrewDog's punk collapsed when behavior contradicted brand

### Making "Honest" Creatively Interesting (Not Earnest/Boring)

- **Oatly:** Irreverent, provocative, "Brainwashing" — honesty with wit
- **Patagonia:** Counterintuitive ("Don't Buy") — honesty as surprise
- **Basecamp:** Confrontational ("less bullshit") — honesty as provocation
- **Avoid:** Preachiness, corporate responsibility tone, "we care" without proof

## For Byron Sharp: Mental Availability vs. PR

### Key Distinction

- **Mental availability:** "Probability that a buyer will notice, recognize and/or think of a brand in buying situations" — depends on memory structures linked to buying occasions (Byron Sharp)
- **Not the same as awareness:** Varies by context; about accessibility in memory, not attitude
- **Distinctive assets:** Colors, shapes, sounds, characters, pack forms — non-verbal cues that identify brand without logo

### Which Adjacent Cases Built Mental Availability?

| Brand | PR/Fame | Mental Availability | Distinctive Assets |
|-------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Patagonia** | High (Don't Buy, Earth shareholder) | High — linked to "outdoor + responsibility" CEPs | Fitz Roy logo, earth palette, Worn Wear |
| **Oatly** | High (provocative ads) | Moderate — packaging as main asset; typeface distinctive | Toni Grotesk, irreverent pack design |
| **Cards Against Humanity** | Very high (stunts) | Low for *product* — fame about brand personality, not buying occasion | Black box, card format |
| **BrewDog** | Very high (controversy) | High during peak — Punk IPA, stencil aesthetic | Later undermined by behavior |

### PR Stunt vs. Cultural Penetration

- **PR stunt:** One-off spike in awareness; no durable memory structure for category entry; no distinctive asset reinforcement
- **Cultural penetration:** Brand linked to multiple Category Entry Points; assets reinforced across touchpoints; buying situation triggers brand retrieval
- **Dove, Always:** Sustained campaigns + distinctive assets (Dove Real Beauty imagery, #LikeAGirl phrase) = cultural penetration with commercial results
- **Cards Against Humanity:** Stunts built fame but not consistent CEP linkage — people think of brand for "outrage" or "Black Friday," not "game night"

### Implication for Second Chair

- **Lead gen vendor CEPs:** "Need more PI leads," "Google too expensive," "Lead source dried up," "Trying new vendor"
- **Mental availability requires:** Consistent presence when attorneys are in those moments (PILMMA, AAJ, Google search, referral conversation)
- **Distinctive assets:** Visual/verbal elements that persist across campaigns — not one campaign, but system
- **Avoid:** PR wins that don't reinforce memory structures (e.g., single stunt that doesn't link to "when I need leads" occasions)
