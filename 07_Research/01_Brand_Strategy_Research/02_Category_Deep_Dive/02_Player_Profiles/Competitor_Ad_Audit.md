## Part 4: The Eight Documented Misleading Claim Patterns

The following claim types have been documented across multiple vendors with named attorney attestation:

**Pattern 1: The Fake Government Program**
*"New 2025 Compensation Program"* â€” implies a governmental or industry benefit program. Buried disclaimer: "not affiliated with any governmental agency." Year-rolling tactic documented (2024 â†’ 2025).

**Pattern 2: The Fake AI Settlement Calculator**
AI tool claiming to calculate exact settlement value. Reality: no such insurer payout database exists. Documented by Darl Champion with specific counter-evidence.

**Pattern 3: Actor Testimonials**
Videos featuring people describing large settlements. Reality: actors, not real clients. Prohibited under California SB 37 (effective 2026).

**Pattern 4: Impossible Timelines**
"Get your payout in weeks" / "Huge compensation in 30 days." Reality: significant PI settlements almost never resolve in 30 days.

**Pattern 5: False Free Service**
"Free case evaluation" implies no cost. Reality: the consumer's data is sold; they pay a contingency percentage on any recovery.

**Pattern 6: Insurer-Conspiracy Framing**
"Insurance companies don't want you to know this" / "find out what the insurers are hiding." Creates false narrative of entitlements being concealed.

**Pattern 7: Inflated Settlement Showcases**
Cherry-picked outlier cases ($650Kâ€“$1.25M) displayed as typical outcomes on lead capture pages.

**Pattern 8: Coached Lead Fraud**
Vendors (often offshore) instruct leads on what symptoms to claim, provide scripted intake answers, and in some cases supply doctored medical records. Documented at Mass Torts Made Perfect conference with screenshots.

## Part 5: Regulatory Exposure Summary

| Rule / Regulation | What It Covers | Who's At Risk |
|-------------------|---------------|---------------|
| ABA Rule 7.1 | No false/misleading communications | Attorneys buying leads from vendors running deceptive consumer ads |
| ABA Rule 7.3 | No live solicitation of specific individuals | Vendors doing targeted outreach to accident victims post-incident |
| ABA Rule 5.4 | No fee-splitting with non-lawyers | "Pay per signed retainer" models â€” under regulatory review in Louisiana and other states |
| FCC One-to-One Consent (eff. 1/27/2025) | Each firm must obtain separate consumer consent | All shared-lead vendors |
| California SB 37 (eff. 1/1/2026) | No guaranteed results; real attorney names required; private right of action | Any vendor running anonymous consumer lead gen in California |
| Texas Barratry | No cold-solicitation of accident victims using police reports/scraped data | Vendors using accident data for outbound targeting |
| Florida Bar | Leads must come from Qualifying Providers; 30-day post-accident contact restriction | Any vendor targeting Florida consumers within 30 days of incident |

**Vicarious liability (documented across bar guidance):** Attorneys bear full bar responsibility for conduct their lead gen vendors engage in on their behalf. The firm's license is at risk, not the vendor's.
