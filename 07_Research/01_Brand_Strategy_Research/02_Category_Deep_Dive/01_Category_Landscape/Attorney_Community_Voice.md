## Part 4: What Attorneys Are Saying About the Advertising Itself

This is the highest-signal section for Second Chair's positioning. The attorneys who are most vocal are not upset about lead prices or conversion rates. They're upset about the advertising. And they're connecting it directly to their professional identity.

### The Core Statement of the Problem

**Ryan McKeen, verbatim:**
> *"What lawyer wants a client with those expectations?"*

**Darl Champion, verbatim:**
> *"Do you really want to start off the attorney-client relationship that way?"*

**Barrett Robin, verbatim:**
> *"If tort reformers really want to rein things in, they ought to go after these companies that are encouraging bogus claims."*

These are not abstract ethics concerns. They're practice management concerns. The advertising sets client expectations. Misleading advertising creates clients with wrong expectations. Wrong-expectation clients generate complaints, malpractice risk, and professional shame. Attorneys know this. And they can't opt out of it if they buy leads without reviewing the advertising.

No attorney on record says: *"I don't like these ads because they violate Rule 7.1."* That's the legal frame. The human frame is different.

These are identity words. An attorney who feels like a snakeoil salesman has lost something beyond a marketing budget. They've lost the self-concept of the lawyer they wanted to be. That's Jon Steel's insight made concrete: the category has taken the good feeling about how attorneys get clients. The attorneys who speak up most loudly are the ones who feel it most acutely.
