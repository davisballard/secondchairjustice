## WHAT WE ARE SOLVING

Second Chair is a PI (personal injury) lead generation company. It sells leads to PI attorneys — primarily firms specializing in auto accident, slip-and-fall, mass tort, and medical malpractice cases. Second Chair generates those leads through paid media (primarily Meta, supplemented by Google) and sells them on a subscription or cost-per-lead basis to attorney clients.

The client-facing dashboard is the product the PI attorney logs into to:
1. See the performance of their Second Chair account
2. Monitor lead volume, quality, and conversion
3. Understand cost efficiency (cost per lead, cost per signed case)
4. Track trends over time
5. Make decisions about their subscription, spend, and territory coverage
6. Present performance data internally (to partners, to their firm's management)

This dashboard is not a CRM. It does not replace the attorney's case management software. It is a **performance intelligence product** — the analytical layer that proves Second Chair delivers what it promises.

## WHY THE DASHBOARD IS A BRAND PROBLEM, NOT JUST A PRODUCT PROBLEM

The visual system built in `14_Visual_Identity/` creates a promise: Second Chair is a different category of partner than every PI lead gen vendor the attorney has ever used. The analog collateral — the letterhead, the business card, the printed proposal — makes that promise through form before a word is read.

The dashboard is where the promise is either kept or broken.

If the attorney signs based on the analog brand signal and logs into a generic SaaS dashboard with navy sidebar navigation, rounded card corners, and stat blocks in Arial — the brand has failed. The visual trust built by the physical collateral collapses at first login.

The dashboard must therefore:
- Match the register of the analog collateral (same typefaces, same color system, same Exhibit convention, same rule weights)
- Exceed the functional quality of every competitor tool (not just aesthetically — structurally: does it answer the questions that matter?)
- Do both without sacrificing either

This is the synthesis test from `06_Digital_Applications.md`: authoritative and modern first. Era-intelligent craft on closer inspection. If either fails, the design has not succeeded.

## THE PRIMARY USER

**Who:** A PI attorney or their designated admin (office manager, paralegal) at a firm with 1–10 attorneys. Primarily solo practitioners and small firm partners.

**When they log in:** Monday morning, before the week starts. Or end of month, reviewing performance. Or immediately after a case signs, checking their pipeline. Occasionally in real-time during a busy intake period.

**What they want to know (in order of priority):**
1. How many leads did I get? Are they good?
2. How much is this costing me per signed case?
3. Am I on track relative to last month / last quarter?
4. Are there any issues I need to address (lead quality complaints, billing, coverage gaps)?
5. Is my investment with Second Chair working?

**What they do NOT want:**
- To be taught how to read a chart
- To scroll through unstructured data looking for the thing that matters
- To see the same stat block layout they see in every other legal vendor's portal
- To wonder if the numbers are accurate

**Their baseline:** These attorneys are data-literate in a legal sense — they read briefs, analyze deposition transcripts, build factual arguments. They are not data scientists. They understand trend lines, percentages, and comparisons. They do not need animated charts or interactive data exploration. They need clear answers.

## THE SUCCESS CRITERIA

**Functional:** An attorney can answer all five of their primary questions (see above) within 60 seconds of opening the dashboard. No scrolling required for the first three questions. No clicks required for the first two.

**Architectural:** Every screen can be described in one sentence as the question it answers. Every exhibit is numbered and its title states a conclusion, not a subject. The information hierarchy matches the analytical hierarchy.

**Visual:** Every screen passes Graham Fink's test — it reads as designed by someone serious, not generated by a template. Every screen passes the synthesis test — authoritative and modern first; era-intelligent craft on closer inspection.

**System integrity:** Zero violations of `14_Visual_Identity/06_Digital_Applications.md`. If a UX requirement creates pressure on the spec, the architecture changes — not the spec.

## THE MASSIMO CONSTRAINT (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

*Massimo speaks:*

*"I will say this once. The visual system exists. The typefaces, the rule weights, the exhibit convention, the color system — these are not aesthetic preferences to be negotiated based on what is easier to build or easier to use. They are the brand. A dashboard built with the wrong typeface, the wrong rule weight, or a floating legend box is not a Second Chair dashboard — it is a competitor's dashboard with a Second Chair logo on it.*

*Luke is excellent at his work. He has the right instincts. But product design creates pressure toward compromise: 'the spec says 0.5pt rule but the developer said that's hard to do so we'll use 1px which is basically the same.' It is not the same. The discipline that makes this system work is the discipline that says no to 'basically the same.'*

*I will review every screen specification before Figma execution begins. Every component. Every rule weight. Every exhibit label. If the visual system has been compromised anywhere, we start that section again.*"
