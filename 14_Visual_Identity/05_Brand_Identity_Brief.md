# Second Chair — Brand Identity Brief
*Final. March 1, 2026.*
*Strategy: Julian Cole + Jon Steel | Visual Identity: Massimo Vignelli + Graham Fink | Creative Direction: John Hegarty*

---

## THE SINGLE SENTENCE

Second Chair looks like the firm a PI attorney always wanted to be part of — before they knew they'd be buying leads to survive.

---

## PART ONE: WHY THIS IDENTITY EXISTS

### The Problem It Solves

Personal injury attorneys are trapped. The economics of PI practice mandate advertising. Advertising — the TV spot, the billboard, the aggressive lead vendor — is the thing that stigmatizes the practice. The very activity they need to grow is the activity that undermines who they believe themselves to be.

Jon Steel's psychographic research documented this in the attorneys' own words:

> *"I never thought I'd be a personal injury lawyer... I was embarrassed."*
> *"Being willing to go on TV like a doofus."*
> *"The public generally has a bad image of plaintiff personal injury lawyers."*

The brand that serves these attorneys must do one thing before it does anything else: **restore their professional identity.** Not perform it. Restore it.

### What Restores Identity

In 1977, the Supreme Court ruled that attorney advertising bans were unconstitutional (*Bates v. State Bar*). Before that ruling, prestige law was expressed entirely through non-advertising means: the letterhead, the building directory, the annual report, the binding of the volumes on the shelf. These objects communicated authority because they had to. They were the only communication channel available.

Those objects still exist in the nervous systems of every attorney who went through law school. The West Federal Reporter. The law review. The brass nameplate. The mahogany office. They are not nostalgia — they are the attorney's professional self-concept, stored in physical form.

Second Chair's visual identity is built from those objects. Not as costume. Not as retro styling. As the authentic visual language of the world PI attorneys aspire to belong to.

### The Tech Trust Inversion

There is a second reason the classical register is the right strategic move — one that goes beyond identity restoration and into competitive positioning.

**Lawyers don't distrust technology. They distrust the visual aesthetic that technology arrived wearing.**

Every product that has burned the PI attorney in the last twenty years — the lead vendor that overpromised, the CRM that was abandoned, the platform that locked them in and under-delivered — arrived in the same costume. Clean sans-serif interface. Bold gradient header. "Book a Demo" CTA. Startup-blue primary color. The visual vocabulary of SaaS.

That aesthetic has accumulated an injury record. Attorneys have learned to read it — not consciously, but viscerally — as a signal for "this will not work as promised." The design itself now precedes the product's failure. The costume has become a warning sign.

Second Chair's classical register routes around that accumulated distrust entirely. It does not arrive in the costume of the category that has burned them. It arrives in the visual language of the things they trust most completely and unconditionally: the West Federal Reporter on the shelf, the law review on the desk, the partner's annual report, the brass nameplate at the entrance.

Those objects have never lied to them. That visual language carries zero injury history.

**The bridge: trusted frame, modern content.**

The classical visual register is the envelope. Inside that trusted envelope is everything that belongs to the present: live data, AI-generated insights, CRM dashboards, performance analytics, animated metrics. Modern capabilities expressed in the visual language of maximum credibility.

This is not contradictory. It is the point. The attorney sees: *these people understand my world, they speak my visual language, they have taken the aesthetic of my profession seriously enough to learn it correctly.* That recognition creates the trust that precedes any product interaction. The product then has to deliver — but it gets the chance to, because the trust was not destroyed at first glance.

**Why this territory is defensible.**

No VC-backed legal tech startup will reverse into this territory. Doing so requires genuinely understanding the culture, not just targeting the market. Modern SaaS design culture actively penalizes this aesthetic — product teams pursuing Stripe-like minimalism or the current crypto-clean register would treat this direction as retrograde and unsellable. They cannot copy it even if they wanted to, because their design culture prevents them from recognizing its value.

The wider creative culture is also circling back toward craft, analog, and the made object — in architecture, fashion, graphic design, film. Second Chair captures this instinct in a B2B context where it has never been deployed. The timing is structural, not accidental.

**The constraint this creates.**

Every Second Chair creative decision — website, dashboard, email, deck, report — must pass a single test: *does this look like something a lawyer would reach for when something matters?* Not "does this look modern." Not "does this look designed." Does it look like the thing they trust when the stakes are real.

If it passes that test in a classical visual language, and the data inside it is animated, modern, and accurate, Second Chair has no visual peer in the category. That is a permanent competitive position.

---

### The Aspiration Is Federal

The most important insight from the color palette exploration:

The West Publishing Company produced two primary series. The **state reporters** — forest green cloth, gold lettering — covered each jurisdiction's decisions. The **federal reporters** — oxblood burgundy cloth, gold lettering — covered the circuits, the Supreme Court, the highest courts in the land.

Every PI attorney works in state court. Every PI attorney dreams of the federal case. The landmark case. The one that gets cited. The one that changes things.

Second Chair's palette is **oxblood and gold** — not because it looks good, but because it is the color of aspiration in the legal profession. It is what attorneys reach for when they want to feel that the work they do matters at the highest possible level.

This is the strategic function of the color: it signals to the PI attorney, before any copy is read, that Second Chair understands where they want to go — not just where they are.

## PART TWO: THE VISUAL WORLD

### Where The Brand Lives

The brand world was validated through visual exploration in March 2026. Every image below represents a physical environment or object that Second Chair's identity draws from. These are not mood board aspirations — they are the source material.

**The Law Library**
Floor-to-ceiling dark mahogany shelving. West Federal Reporter volumes in oxblood binding with gold lettering filling every shelf. A single brass banker's lamp throwing warm amber light across the spines. An oxblood leather chair at the reading table. 2700K color temperature — warm incandescent, never fluorescent. This is the room where serious work is done and has been done for a century.

*What it gives the brand:* The environment of accumulated authority. The visual proof that someone has done the work.

**The Brass Nameplate**
A polished brass rectangular plate mounted on dark wood paneling. "SC" engraved in classical Roman letterforms within a thin rectangular border. Aged brass — not bright, not new. The patina of permanence. Found at the entrance to a law office that has been on that floor for thirty years.

*What it gives the brand:* A standalone mark that communicates permanence before anyone reads the name. The monogram form for physical applications.

**The Building Directory**
Dark lobby panel. Individual brass letter plates for each firm — each name in serif capitals within a ruled rectangle. Marble floor catching the light from a brass wall sconce. The directory of firms that have been here long enough to be on the wall.

*What it gives the brand:* The register of institutional legitimacy. Being on the directory means you belong.

**The Journal Title Page**
Cream aged cotton-rag paper. A large bold display serif typeface — high contrast, classical proportions — printing the publication title in two lines. A thin deep burgundy hairline rule running full width between the headline and subtitle. Small caps for the subtitle below. The letterpress impression visible in the paper surface. Warm charcoal ink, not pure black. The page of a publication that has been taken seriously for decades.

*What it gives the brand:* The typographic register. This is the direct reference for the Second Chair wordmark.

**The Cotton-Rag Paper**
Macro letterpress on Crane Lettra. The paper fiber visible under raking light. The ink sitting IN the surface, not on top. The 0.5pt burgundy rule between typographic elements. This is the craft standard — the quality level that everything Second Chair produces should aspire to, even when produced digitally.

*What it gives the brand:* The texture reference. The proof that craft is visible and matters.

### The Color Temperature of the Brand World

Every environment above is lit at **2700–3000K warm incandescent**. There is no cool light, no fluorescent, no LED white in Second Chair's world. The color temperature is amber. It is the color of a reading lamp, not an office park. This informs all photography direction: warm, amber, available light where possible.

## PART THREE: THE COLOR PALETTE

### Primary Palette

**Oxblood Burgundy — `#6B1A1A`**
- Pantone starting point: 202 C (calibrate against physical West Federal Reporter spine)
- Physical origin: West Federal Reporter binding cloth; Harvard Law Review buckram binding; oxblood leather chair
- Strategic meaning: The Federal series. The Supreme Court. The circuit courts. The aspiration of every PI attorney who went to law school wanting to argue the important cases.
- Usage: The rule (0.5pt horizontal line throughout all applications), single chart accent in data visualization, physical foil on covers, structural elements in digital interfaces
- Category status: **Zero competitors use this color.** The entire PI lead gen category is navy, grey, white, and red. Oxblood is permanently unclaimed territory.

**Old Gold / Brass — `#B8962E`**
- Pantone starting point: 872 C metallic (for offset); 109 C (non-metallic spot color)
- Physical origin: Brass door placards; West Reporter spine lettering; engraved nameplate lettering; Crane & Co. letterhead embossing
- Strategic meaning: The material of permanence. Brass doesn't rust. It ages into authority.
- Usage: Physical applications only — foil stamping, letterpress, engraved collateral, the SC monogram nameplate. In digital: decorative rule lines on cream, never as a field color or text color.
- The test: Aged brass, not polished. It should look like the nameplate on a 1987 firm's door, not a new trophy.

**Cream / Ivory — `#F5F0E8`**
- Pantone starting point: 9183 C (calibrate against Crane Lettra 110lb in physical production)
- Paper stock: Crane Lettra 110lb Cover (cards/letterhead); Mohawk Superfine Ultrawhite Eggshell 80lb Text (documents)
- Physical origin: Cotton-rag letterhead; Harvard Law Review paper stock; West Reporter text block
- Strategic meaning: The opposite of every competitor's background. The PI lead gen category uses white or dark backgrounds. Cream reads as cotton-rag letterhead. It communicates material quality before a word is read.
- Usage: **Primary background for all Second Chair surfaces** — documents, website, decks, letterhead, business cards. Never use pure white (`#FFFFFF`) as a Second Chair background.
- Category status: **Zero PI lead gen vendors use cream.** Immediate differentiation at the background level.

**Warm Charcoal — `#2C2C2C`**
- Physical origin: Quality offset printing on cream paper — ink that has absorbed into the surface
- Strategic meaning: The color of serious printing. Not harsh pure black, not faded grey. The color of something that was made properly.
- Usage: All body text, headlines, wordmark on cream backgrounds. Not pure black — the warmth matters against cream.

### Secondary Palette

**Oxford Navy — `#1B2A4A`** — Foreground structural elements only. Never as a field/background color.
**Forest Green — `#1E4D2B`** — Secondary variant. The state reporter palette. Available for geographic or market-specific applications. Not the primary identity.
**Warm Grey — `#8C8680`** — Supporting text: captions, exhibit labels, footnotes, running heads.

### The Color Exclusion List (Non-Negotiable)

No blue of any kind. No red. No orange. No purple. No gradients. No color not derivable from a physical object in the prestige legal world. These exclusions are permanent — not style preferences, strategic requirements.

### The Physical / Digital Flip

The brand has two modes that use the same colors differently:

| Context | Background | Text/Mark | Accent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Physical** (book cover, foil, plaque) | Oxblood cloth / brass | Gold foil lettering | Gold rule |
| **Digital / Print** (web, letterhead, deck) | Cream `#F5F0E8` | Charcoal `#2C2C2C` | Burgundy `#6B1A1A` rule |

The physical world is dark with gold. The document world is cream with charcoal and burgundy. Both are the same brand. The West Reporter works the same way — oxblood spine with gold lettering on the outside, cream pages with charcoal type on the inside.

## PART FOUR: THE WORDMARK

### Direction: C — The Masthead

The Second Chair wordmark is a two-line typographic mark. It does not have a separate symbol or icon as its primary form — the typography IS the identity, as it was for every prestige law journal that had been publishing for decades before anyone thought to design a logo for it.

### The Structure

```
SECOND
──────────────────
Chair
```

- **Line 1:** "SECOND" in Tiempos Headline Bold — large, dominant, the editorial voice
- **Divider:** 0.5pt Burgundy `#6B1A1A` horizontal rule, full width of "SECOND"
- **Line 2:** "Chair" in Adobe Caslon Pro Regular — approximately 45% the visual size of "SECOND", the institutional anchor

### Why This Structure Works

"SECOND" is large because it earns attention. It is the editorial statement — the publication title, the voice that stops the attorney before they've read the name.

"CHAIR" is smaller because it is the qualifier. In legal practice, the second chair is the attorney who prepares everything — who knows the case inside out, who makes the first chair possible. "Chair" in smaller type is not weakness — it is precision. The brand knows what it is and doesn't need to shout it.

The rule between them is the burgundy 0.5pt line that appears everywhere in Second Chair's visual system: under the letterhead name, between sections in a document, as the single chart accent, on the business card. The rule is the brand's visual signature as much as any letterform.

### The Reference Image

The image that confirmed this direction in the March 2026 exploration session:

> *"American Jurisprudence / A Quarterly Review" — open journal, cream aged cotton-rag paper, large bold classical serif title on two lines, deep burgundy hairline rule between title and subtitle, warm charcoal letterpress ink, raking ambient light.*

Davis's reaction: *"fucking beautiful and amazing and classic, much more emotion and authority than the others."*

That image is the target. "SECOND CHAIR" typeset in this system on a cream background should produce the same emotional response.

### The Typefaces

**Display: Tiempos Headline Bold** (Klim Type Foundry — klim.co.nz)
- Designer: Kris Sowersby
- Character: High-contrast classical Roman proportions. Warm curves with sharp details. Simultaneously historical and unmistakably contemporary. The typeface that could appear on a 1989 AmLaw 100 masthead and a 2026 website without looking out of place in either.
- Used by: Prāvo (legal brand), editorial and institutional publishers
- Why not Century Schoolbook: Century Schoolbook is the Supreme Court's mandated typeface — it communicates the institution. Tiempos Headline communicates the editorial voice that analyzes and interprets the institution. Second Chair is not the court — it is the publication that takes the court seriously.
- License: Purchase at klim.co.nz. Commercial desktop + web license required.

**Body / Wordmark Line 2: Adobe Caslon Pro Regular** (Adobe Fonts)
- Designer: William Caslon I (1720s); digitized by Carol Twombly for Adobe
- Character: Slightly irregular, humanist warmth, beautiful italic, exceptional readability
- Used by: The New Yorker (body text), Declaration of Independence typesetting, Seventh Circuit brief guidelines
- Why Pro version is required: Adobe Caslon Pro includes true optical small caps (not CSS faux small caps), old-style figures, and proper ligatures. The difference is visible to legally trained readers.
- License: Available via Adobe Fonts (Creative Cloud subscription)

### The Monogram Mark

For applications where the full wordmark is too long (favicon, app icon, embossed physical items, wax seal):

**"SC" in a thin rectangular border**
- Typeface: Adobe Caslon Pro, optical small caps
- Border: 0.5pt hairline rectangle — the same pen weight as the wordmark rule
- Color: Oxblood `#6B1A1A` on Cream `#F5F0E8` (primary); Gold `#B8962E` on Oxblood `#6B1A1A` (physical/engraved)
- The form: Engraved brass plaque. The object validated in the March 2026 visual exploration — the brass "SC" nameplate on dark wood paneling.

### Figma Build Sequence

1. Create color styles: `SC/Cream #F5F0E8`, `SC/Burgundy #6B1A1A`, `SC/Charcoal #2C2C2C`, `SC/Gold #B8962E`, `SC/Navy #1B2A4A`, `SC/Green #1E4D2B`, `SC/Grey #8C8680`
2. Set canvas fill to Cream `#F5F0E8`
3. Create text layer: "SECOND" in Tiempos Headline Bold, color `#2C2C2C`
4. Size: Start at 96pt — adjust optically until it fills about 60% of the frame width
5. Create rectangle: width = exact bounding box width of "SECOND" text, height = 0.5px, color `#6B1A1A`, position 8px below the baseline of "SECOND"
6. Create text layer: "Chair" in Adobe Caslon Pro Regular, color `#2C2C2C`, size approximately 45pt (adjust until optically ~45% the visual weight of "SECOND")
7. Position "Chair" flush left, aligned with left edge of "S" in "SECOND"
8. Group all three layers: name "SECOND CHAIR / Wordmark / Primary"
9. Duplicate and test: burgundy background with cream text (reverse lockup)
10. Create the SC monogram mark separately as described above

## PART FIVE: THE TYPOGRAPHY SYSTEM

### The Three Roles

Second Chair's typographic system has three distinct voices, each with a specific job:

**Voice 1 — Institutional Authority (Display)**
Tiempos Headline Bold. The voice that commands attention and communicates that what follows is worth reading. Used for: wordmark, page titles, hero headlines, deck cover headlines, email subject lines.

**Voice 2 — Serious Prose (Body)**
Adobe Caslon Pro Regular. The voice that does the reading work. The voice of the founding document, the law review article, The New Yorker long-form piece. Used for: all body copy, the "Chair" in the wordmark, subtitles, document body.

**Voice 3 — Institutional Precision (UI / Metadata)**
Söhne Regular (Klim Type Foundry). The voice of structure — labels, navigation, captions, running heads, metadata. A refined grotesque that carries warmth without the corporate coldness of Inter or Helvetica. Used for: website navigation, UI labels, caption text, exhibit labels when small caps are unavailable.

### The Type Scale

| Role | Face | Size | Leading | Tracking | Case |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Display / Hero | Tiempos Headline Bold | 48–96pt | 105% | 0 | Title case |
| H1 | Tiempos Headline Bold | 32–48pt | 115% | 0 | Title case |
| H2 | Adobe Caslon Pro Regular | 18–24pt | 125% | 0 | Title case |
| H3 | Adobe Caslon Pro Italic | 14–16pt | 135% | 0 | Title case |
| Body | Adobe Caslon Pro Regular | 10–11pt | 150% | 0 | Sentence case |
| Caption / Label | Söhne Regular | 8–9pt | 130% | +5 | All caps |
| Exhibit Label | Adobe Caslon Pro Small Caps | 7–8pt | 120% | +20 | "EXHIBIT N:" |
| Running Head | Söhne Regular | 7–8pt | 120% | +5 | All caps |
| Footnote | Adobe Caslon Pro Regular | 8–9pt | 130% | 0 | Sentence case |

### The Hierarchy Rule

Color does not create hierarchy. Typography creates hierarchy. A Second Chair document printed in black-and-white on cream paper must have a completely clear and legible hierarchy. If it doesn't, the type isn't working. Color (burgundy) is added for structural elements only — the rule, the chart accent — not to create visual priority.

### The Small Caps Rule

True optical small caps only — the actual small cap glyphs designed by the type designer, accessed via OpenType features in Figma (Advanced Typography → Small Caps). CSS `font-variant: small-caps` on scaled capitals is not acceptable. The difference is visible to legally trained readers. Second Chair's claim to authenticity begins in the letterforms.

### The Exhibit Convention

Every data point, chart, table, or statistical reference in Second Chair communications is labeled:

**EXHIBIT N:** in Adobe Caslon Pro small caps, 8pt, Warm Grey `#8C8680`, left-aligned, above the chart title.

This convention comes from two sources simultaneously:
1. **McKinsey** — the "Exhibit n:" naming system signals analytical rigor and consulting-grade work to any attorney who has seen a McKinsey deck
2. **The courtroom** — in legal practice, an "exhibit" is a formally introduced piece of evidence. Every data point Second Chair presents is evidence, introduced formally.

Zero competitors in the PI lead gen category use this convention. It is completely ownable and builds with every piece of content published.

## PART SIX: THE APPLICATIONS

### Letterhead
- Stock: Crane Lettra 110lb Fluorescent White (the paper IS the cream — do not print cream on white stock)
- Wordmark: Tiempos Headline Bold "SECOND" + Caslon Pro "Chair" + 0.5pt Burgundy rule
- Contact information: Adobe Caslon Pro 9pt, Warm Charcoal, small caps for labels
- Burgundy 0.5pt rule under the wordmark header
- Generous margins: minimum 1.25" on all sides

### Business Cards
- Stock: Crane Lettra 220lb duplex (for rigidity and feel)
- Front: Wordmark (wordmark lockup), name in Caslon Pro 9pt, title in Caslon Pro small caps 8pt, contact in Caslon Pro 8pt
- Engraved/letterpress version: Warm charcoal ink, 0.5pt burgundy rule
- Physical premium version: Thermographed or letterpress on cream; foil variant with gold SC monogram on oxblood back panel

### Website
- Background: Cream `#F5F0E8` — entire site
- Hero: "SECOND" in Tiempos Headline Bold at large display size, 0.5pt Burgundy rule, "Chair" in Caslon Pro below
- Navigation: Söhne Regular, small caps, Warm Charcoal, 0.5pt Burgundy rule below nav bar
- Body: Adobe Caslon Pro 10.5pt / 16pt leading
- No photography in the hero — the typography IS the hero
- Photography appears in content sections: 2700K warm amber, documentary register, environmental (no stock)

### Data Exhibits
- Canvas: Cream `#F5F0E8`
- Exhibit label: "EXHIBIT N:" Caslon Pro small caps, 8pt, Warm Grey, top-left
- Chart title: States the conclusion ("Signed cases cost 40% less at CPSC-aligned vendors") in Tiempos Headline or Caslon Pro Bold
- Highlight color: Burgundy `#6B1A1A` — single accent only
- Supporting data: Warm Grey `#8C8680`
- No chart borders, no legend boxes, no grid lines beyond baseline, no 3D, no gradients
- Direct labels on data points — never a legend

### Photography Direction
- Color temperature: 2700–3000K warm incandescent always
- Register: Documentary, environmental, Burt Glinn / Magnum Photos quality
- Subjects: Environments (offices, libraries, courthouses), not people performing for camera
- B&W: Available as deliberate quality signal (Goldman Sachs annual report register)
- Forbidden: Stock photography, posed lawyer photos, courthouse steps clichés, bright cool light

## PART SEVEN: WHAT THIS IDENTITY IS NOT

Because the visual territory is adjacent to law firm branding, these distinctions matter:

**Not a law firm.** Law firms have crests, shields, and partner portraits. Second Chair has a masthead, an exhibit convention, and a West Reporter spine. The distinction is: law firms communicate history and partnership. Second Chair communicates rigor and craft.

**Not a vendor.** Vendors have stat blocks, "Book a Call" CTAs, and process infographics. Second Chair has white space, Tufte exhibits, and a running head. The distinction is between asking for trust and having already earned it.

**Not nostalgic.** The 1986–1995 aesthetic is not retro styling for Second Chair — it is the authentic visual language of its audience's aspiration. The attorney who responds to this identity does not think "this is old-fashioned." They think "this is serious." The distinction between nostalgia and recognition is the difference between costume and character.

**Not trying.** The worst thing this identity can do is look designed. Everything in this system — the typefaces, the paper color, the rule weight, the exhibit convention — comes from objects and institutions that predate modern brand design. They were not designed. They were built over decades of institutional use. Second Chair inherits their authority by using them correctly, not by imitating them.

## THE HEGARTY CHECK

*"The question I'd ask about this identity: does it feel like it needs approval? Does it look like something that was hoping to be noticed?*

*It doesn't. A West Federal Reporter volume doesn't hope to be noticed. It sits on the shelf and waits for the attorney who needs what's inside. That's the register Second Chair operates in. Not 'look at us.' Just: we are here, we have always been here, and we know exactly what we're doing.*

*When the PI attorney sees this mark, the response shouldn't be 'that's a nice logo.' It should be something closer to recognition — the feeling of seeing your own aspiration named correctly for the first time.*

*That's the test. Does it pass? Yes."*
