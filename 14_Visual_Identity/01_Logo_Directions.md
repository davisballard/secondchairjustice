# Second Chair — Logo Directions
*Session: March 1, 2026 | Massimo Vignelli (lead) + Graham Fink + John Hegarty*

---

## MASSIMO OPENS

*"Three directions. Each has strategic distinction — these are not variations of the same idea, they are genuinely different positions. Each is defensible. Each can be built into a full system. The question is which one feels inevitable for Second Chair specifically.*

*I'll present each with: what it is, what it means, where it lives culturally, exact specifications for Figma, and the Rand diagnostic. Graham will add his visual zag read. Hegarty will bravery-check all three at the end.*

*Before I present — one principle from Rand that I will not compromise on: we present the wordmark in true optical small caps. Not CSS small caps. Not faux small caps generated by scaling capitals. True small cap glyphs, as cut by the type designer. The difference is visible to every person who trained in the prestige legal world. A trained eye can spot faux small caps. If Second Chair's identity is built on authenticity, the letterforms themselves must be authentic. This is a non-negotiable.*"

## DIRECTION A: THE REGISTRY

### The Concept

*"The building directory plate next to the elevator. The brass placard at the entrance to a prestigious firm. The Supreme Court brief cover. Before law firms could advertise, their identity was their name — set properly, in the right weight, with the correct spacing. The name itself was the whole communication.*

*This is a pure wordmark. No mark. No symbol. No embellishment. 'SECOND CHAIR' in Century Schoolbook small caps with a single Burgundy rule. The restraint IS the confidence. The firm that needs no symbol because the name alone carries authority.*"

### Cultural Register

The Supreme Court. The Harvard Law Review. The West Reporter spine. The Goldman Sachs annual report spine. The lobby directory of Sullivan & Cromwell. The letterhead of a firm that has never needed to explain what it does.

### Visual Description (for Figma)

**The Wordmark:**
- Typeface: Century Schoolbook Pro (or Century Schoolbook Regular where Pro unavailable)
- Case treatment: True optical small caps — all glyphs in small cap form. NOT scaled capitals.
- String: `SECOND CHAIR`
- Tracking: +30 units (approximately 300/1000 em in optical tracking terms; in Figma: letter-spacing ~0.12em)
- Weight: Regular (Book). Not Bold. Authority does not shout.
- Size relationship: "SECOND" and "CHAIR" on a single line, separated by a standard word space

**Color:**
- Primary version: Warm Charcoal `#2C2C2C` letterforms on Cream `#F5F0E8` background
- Reverse version: Cream `#F5F0E8` letterforms on Warm Charcoal `#2C2C2C` background
- Engraved version: Old Gold `#B8962E` letterforms on Oxford Navy `#1B2A4A` (physical collateral only — letterpress, foil)

**The Rule:**
- A single hairline horizontal rule in Burgundy `#6B1A1A`
- Weight: 0.5pt (0.5px at 1x)
- Position: Full width of the wordmark, flush left to flush right, sitting 8–10pt below the baseline of the wordmark
- This rule is the only chromatic element in the primary mark — it does not compete with the letterforms, it anchors them

**Spacing system:**
- Clear space above: equal to the cap-height of the wordmark
- Clear space below: equal to the cap-height of the wordmark
- Clear space left and right: equal to the width of one "S"

**Stacked variant (for square applications — avatar, favicon, embossed):**
- "SECOND" on line 1, "CHAIR" on line 2, both in small caps
- "SECOND" at primary size, "CHAIR" flush-left aligned with "S"
- The Burgundy rule runs between the two words, full width of "SECOND"
- A monogram reduction for favicon/app: "SC" in Century Schoolbook small caps, within a hairline square border

**Figma frame spec:**
- Canvas: `#F5F0E8` (Cream)
- Wordmark text layer: Century Schoolbook, Regular, All Small Caps (via OpenType feature)
- Letter-spacing: 0.12em
- Text color: `#2C2C2C`
- Rule: Rectangle, height 0.5px, width = wordmark width, color `#6B1A1A`, positioned 8px below text baseline

### The Rand Diagnostic

| Criterion | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Distinctive** | Pass — Zero competitors use a serif small caps wordmark in this category. The typographic register is completely unoccupied. |
| **Memorable** | Pass — Century Schoolbook small caps carries a specific institutional memory for legally-trained audiences. The Supreme Court files briefs in this face. |
| **Scalable** | Pass — Small caps hold their structure at small sizes better than standard caps. The single-weight rule reads at any scale. |
| **Timeless** | Pass — This mark could appear on a letterhead from 1991 or a website from 2026 with equal credibility. It is not trend-dependent. |
| **Versatile** | Pass — The wordmark works on cream, on navy, on charcoal, embossed, foiled, letterpress. The rule is the only chromatic element, making color flexibility straightforward. |
| **Simple** | Pass — Describable in one sentence: "The firm name in Supreme Court small caps with a Burgundy rule." |
| **Meaningful** | Pass — The form embodies Second Chair's brand truth. The typeface is the Supreme Court standard. The small caps read as institutional precision, not decorative choice. |
| **Ownable** | Pass — No competitor can claim Century Schoolbook small caps. The distinctive asset can be locked immediately. |

### Graham's Read

*"The visual zag here is total. The category wordmark convention is bold grotesque sans, often lowercase, often blue. This is the exact opposite: serif, small caps, warm charcoal on cream. And the rule — that 0.5pt Burgundy line — does something specific. It's not decorative. It reads like a legal document header rule. A brief cover. The attorney who's submitted a brief to a federal court has seen this rule ten thousand times. The visual/verbal marriage here isn't with a headline — it's with the attorney's own professional identity. The mark marries the brand name to the visual language of their training.*

*If someone showed me this without context, I'd think: law firm, serious, established. No one would think: lead gen vendor. That's a successful visual zag.*"

### Hegarty's Check

*"The bravery of Direction A is the bravery of restraint. No symbol. No embellishment. Just the name, set perfectly, in the right typeface. The cowardly version of this is to add something — a mark, a crest, a decorative element — to 'make it feel more complete.' The brave version trusts that the name, set with complete conviction, is sufficient.*

*The question I'd ask Davis: Do you trust the name to carry the identity? 'Second Chair' is a genuinely evocative name — it has meaning in the legal world (the attorney who prepares the case), it's humble, and it's specific. A pure wordmark bets everything on the name being strong enough. I believe it is.*

*Verdict on Direction A: It passes. But we must execute the small caps correctly. Faux small caps would invalidate the entire claim to authenticity. The craftsmanship must be real.*"

## DIRECTION B: THE PLAQUE

### The Concept

*"The engraved brass door plate. The ex libris bookplate. The building lobby nameplate with the gold letters pressed into dark material. This direction adds a typographic mark — a formal 'SC' monogram — to the wordmark, giving the system a device that can function as a standalone mark for applications where the full wordmark is too long.*

*The distinction from Direction A: this creates a two-element system. The wordmark (used for primary brand communication) and the monogram mark (used for favicon, embossed physical items, app icon, the stamp on a document). They are the same typeface — Century Schoolbook — so the system has unity through the typeface rather than through arbitrary graphic elements.*"

### Cultural Register

The engraved nameplate on a partner's office door. The ex libris stamp inside a law school casebook. The seal pressed into correspondence wax. The Crane & Co. letterhead monogram. The engraved calling card. This is the register of the thing that was made once, properly, and lasts.

### Visual Description (for Figma)

**The Monogram Mark:**
- Typeface: Century Schoolbook Pro, Regular weight, optical small caps
- String: `SC`
- Size: The mark should be optically the same as two small-cap letters from the wordmark
- Letter-spacing: Standard (0em) — the two letters sit in natural relationship, not artificially spaced
- Border: A hairline rectangular rule enclosing the "SC", leaving equal internal padding on all four sides (padding = approximately 50% of the cap-height of the "S")
- Rule weight: 0.5pt (matching the wordmark rule weight — the same pen, the same hand)
- Color: Mark in Burgundy `#6B1A1A`, border in Burgundy `#6B1A1A`
- Background: Cream `#F5F0E8`
- Alternative: Mark in Old Gold `#B8962E` on Oxford Navy `#1B2A4A` (physical collateral — engraved quality)

**Why Century Schoolbook for the monogram (not Bodoni):**
Century Schoolbook's ball terminals and wide proportions give the "S" and "C" letterforms a weight and openness that read well at small sizes and in engraved form. Bodoni would be tempting for the high contrast aesthetic, but at small sizes the hairline strokes disappear. Century holds. The system unity is also critical — the monogram and the wordmark are the same typeface.

**The Wordmark (identical to Direction A with one modification):**
- "SECOND CHAIR" in Century Schoolbook small caps, +30 tracking, `#2C2C2C` on `#F5F0E8`
- The Burgundy 0.5pt rule below the wordmark is maintained

**Combination lockup (primary):**
- Monogram mark sits to the left of the wordmark
- Vertical alignment: The base of the rectangular border aligns with the baseline of the wordmark
- Horizontal separation: One wordmark "S" width of space between the right edge of the border and the first letter of the wordmark
- The Burgundy rule extends from below the wordmark to a position 8pt below the baseline — it does NOT extend under the monogram mark. The mark and wordmark each have their own spatial logic.

**Secondary lockup (stacked):**
- Monogram mark centered above the wordmark
- Wordmark centered below (center-aligned)
- Spacing: 12pt between base of mark border and cap-height of wordmark
- The rule runs full width of the wordmark, not the mark

**Standalone mark (favicon/app icon/embossed):**
- The "SC" monogram within the rectangular border at any size
- At favicon sizes (16px, 32px): The rectangle simplifies to the "SC" letterforms only, without border, or with a very simplified border at 1px
- For physical embossing/debossing: The rectangular plaque form works perfectly — it is literally the form of an engraved plate

**Figma frame spec:**
- Monogram: Text layer "SC", Century Schoolbook Regular, All Small Caps (OpenType), letter-spacing 0em
- Border: Auto Layout frame around text, padding 50% of cap height, border 0.5px color `#6B1A1A`, fill none
- Combination lockup: Auto Layout, horizontal, gap = width of "S" character
- Wordmark: Identical to Direction A spec

### The Rand Diagnostic

| Criterion | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Distinctive** | Pass — The rectangular plaque format with typographic monogram is unused in category. The building-directory quality is specific. |
| **Memorable** | Pass — The "SC" in a bordered rectangle is a simple, reducible unit. One viewing is sufficient to recall the form. |
| **Scalable** | Pass — The monogram mark reduces cleanly to 16px favicon. The rectangular border at very small sizes becomes a square with letterforms. The wordmark behaves identically to Direction A. |
| **Timeless** | Pass — The engraved plaque form predates modern design. It will not feel dated. |
| **Versatile** | Pass — Two-element system (mark + wordmark) gives flexibility. Mark alone works for favicon, embossing, wax seal, app icon. Wordmark alone works for letterhead, running head, business card first line. |
| **Simple** | Pass — "SC in a ruled rectangle, with the wordmark." Describable in one sentence. |
| **Meaningful** | Pass — The rectangular border references the physical plaque. It communicates material, permanence, craft. |
| **Ownable** | Pass — Specific to Second Chair. The combination of Century Schoolbook small caps in a rectangular border with this color system belongs to no one else in the category. |

### Graham's Read

*"This direction gives us a weapon that Direction A doesn't have: a standalone mark. Direction A is strong, but every attorney's business card, every app icon, every embossed notebook cover — you need something that works in a square format. The 'SC' in the border solves that.*

*The risk with Direction B is execution discipline. The monogram mark has to be held tightly. If someone scales the border wrong, or adjusts the padding inconsistently, the plaque quality disappears. This direction requires strict system rules to stay authentic. Massimo's job is to make those rules so clear that no one can deviate without knowing they're deviating.*

*Typography observation: I'd push for seeing the 'SC' with slightly more internal letter-spacing than standard — not much, maybe +5 units — to give the two letters breathing room within the border. The border is a container; the letters should fill it with authority, not crowd it.*"

### Hegarty's Check

*"Direction B is the one most attorneys will find familiar. The branded document, the engraved plate, the stamped correspondence — this form has existed in professional service for 150 years. There's safety in that familiarity.*

*But I'll push back slightly: the safety is also the risk. The monogram mark format is not unusual in professional services. Every law firm in the AmLaw 100 has some version of an 'SC' or 'WC' or 'TH' mark in a box. What makes this one different?*

*The answer is: the typeface, the color system, and the restraint. The Century Schoolbook small caps in that border with that Burgundy read completely differently from a standard law firm monogram in Helvetica with navy. The answer is correct — the differentiation is in the execution, not the form.*

*Verdict on Direction B: It passes. But the system rules must be iron. And the standalone mark needs to be tested at 16px before we commit. I've seen plaque-style marks fall apart at favicon size.*"

## DIRECTION C: THE MASTHEAD

### The Concept

*"The Harvard Law Review masthead as redesigned by Upstatement for 2024. The American Lawyer before it abandoned its identity. A law journal from 1991 that someone kept because it was designed beautifully.*

*This direction uses two typefaces in deliberate contrast. 'SECOND' in Tiempos Headline Bold — the contemporary editorial voice, the modern legal publication, 2026. 'CHAIR' in Adobe Caslon Pro Regular — the institutional anchor, the founding document face, 1776–1991.*

*The logic is strategic, not decorative. The two words perform the brand's dual identity: Second Chair is a new company (SECOND — editorial, present) that speaks from the prestige legal tradition (CHAIR — institutional, permanent). The visual contrast between the two words enacts the synthesis the brand is trying to achieve.*"

### Cultural Register

The masthead of a law journal with an excellent art director. The American Lawyer circa 1991. The Harvard Law Review digital redesign (Upstatement, 2024). Bloomberg Law's editorial voice. The moment when legal publishing stopped looking institutional and started looking designed — but without losing authority.

This is the register of the modern classic: the institution that knows its history and knows how to present it for contemporary audiences.

### Visual Description (for Figma)

**The Wordmark:**
- Line 1: "SECOND" in Tiempos Headline Bold (Klim Type Foundry)
  - Case: Title case — standard mixed case, NOT small caps. The editorial voice is not in small caps.
  - Tracking: Standard (0em — Tiempos sets beautifully at default tracking)
  - Size: This is the dominant element — set the scale here first
  - Color: Warm Charcoal `#2C2C2C`

- Line 2: "CHAIR" in Adobe Caslon Pro Regular
  - Case: Standard mixed case
  - Size: Approximately 50–55% of the point size of "SECOND" — this is the key proportion. The size differential must read as intentional editorial hierarchy, not inconsistency. Test: at the selected sizes, does "CHAIR" read as a subhead or as co-equal? It should read as subhead.
  - Tracking: +10–15 units (slightly more open than standard to match the weight of "SECOND" visually across the size difference)
  - Color: Burgundy `#6B1A1A`
  - Alignment: Flush-left, aligned with the left edge of the "S" in "SECOND"

**The Rule:**
- A 0.5pt Burgundy `#6B1A1A` horizontal rule
- Position: Between "SECOND" and "CHAIR", sitting on the baseline of "SECOND"
- Width: Exactly the width of "SECOND" (not the width of "CHAIR", not full-width — it belongs to "SECOND")
- This rule is a descender line — it marks where the first word ends and the second begins

**Sizing proportion guide (Figma):**
- If "SECOND" is set at 48pt, set "CHAIR" at approximately 26pt
- If "SECOND" is set at 72pt, set "CHAIR" at approximately 38pt
- The optical test: Stand back from the screen. "SECOND" should dominate. "CHAIR" should read as the precise institutional qualifier that earns "SECOND" the right to be large.

**Color:**
- Primary: Warm Charcoal `#2C2C2C` (SECOND) + Burgundy `#6B1A1A` (CHAIR) on Cream `#F5F0E8`
- Secondary: Cream `#F5F0E8` (SECOND) + Old Gold `#B8962E` (CHAIR) on Warm Charcoal `#2C2C2C` or Oxford Navy `#1B2A4A`

**Monogram reduction for Direction C:**
- This direction is the least straightforward to reduce to a favicon/app mark
- Options: (1) A typographic "2C" — "2" in Tiempos, "C" in Caslon, the numeral playing on "second" and "chair"; (2) Simply "S" in Tiempos as the standalone mark; (3) A rectangular border enclosing "SC" in mixed typefaces (Tiempos "S" + Caslon "C")
- Recommendation: Option 2 (single "S" in Tiempos Bold) for its simplicity and distinctive letterform

**Figma frame spec:**
- Layer 1 (SECOND): Text layer, Tiempos Headline Bold, standard case, 0em letter-spacing, `#2C2C2C`
- Layer 2 (CHAIR): Text layer, Adobe Caslon Pro Regular, standard case, letter-spacing 0.04em, `#6B1A1A`, flush left to "S"
- Layer 3 (Rule): Rectangle, 0.5px height, width = bounding box of "SECOND" text layer, `#6B1A1A`, positioned on baseline of SECOND layer
- Frame: Auto Layout vertical, gap = 4px, left-aligned
- Canvas: `#F5F0E8`

**Font sourcing:**
- Tiempos Headline: Available at klim.co.nz — Klim Type Foundry, Bold weight
- Adobe Caslon Pro: Available in Adobe Fonts (Typekit) — Pro version required for true small caps (if small cap variant desired in body system)

### The Rand Diagnostic

| Criterion | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Distinctive** | Pass — No competitor uses a two-typeface editorial wordmark. The split between display and body typefaces in a single wordmark is unusual and visually arresting. |
| **Memorable** | Pass — The visual contrast between the large bold "SECOND" and the smaller Caslon "CHAIR" creates a hierarchy that is easy to recall. The arrangement is specific. |
| **Scalable** | Conditional — The two-typeface wordmark is readable at medium and large sizes. At small sizes (below 80px wide), the size relationship between "SECOND" and "CHAIR" begins to compress and the distinction loses force. This direction requires a strong standalone mark for small applications. |
| **Timeless** | Pass — Tiempos Headline is a contemporary typeface, but it is designed to feel timeless. Caslon is 300 years old. Together they create a temporal tension that reads as intentional and won't feel dated. |
| **Versatile** | Conditional — The two-color wordmark (charcoal + burgundy) requires care in environments where color control is limited. A single-color version (all charcoal) is required as a reduction. |
| **Simple** | Conditional — Describable but requires more words: "A two-typeface wordmark with 'SECOND' in bold display and 'CHAIR' in smaller serif." More complex than Directions A or B. |
| **Meaningful** | Pass — The form embodies the brand's dual identity. The typeface contrast enacts the synthesis between contemporary perspective and institutional authority. |
| **Ownable** | Pass — The specific pairing of Tiempos Headline with Adobe Caslon Pro in this size relationship is ownable. No competitor is near this territory. |

### Graham's Read

*"Direction C is the one I find most visually interesting as an art director. The size differential between 'SECOND' and 'CHAIR' creates a visual tension that the other two directions don't have. There's something happening compositionally — the large 'SECOND' makes you commit to reading, then 'CHAIR' grounds it. It's a small visual journey.*

*The masthead reference is right. This looks like it belongs at the top of a publication, not a vendor website. That's a strong zag — a publication has a different relationship with its audience than a vendor does. A publication is authoritative. A publication informs. A vendor sells.*

*One thing to watch: Tiempos Headline in Bold has a personality. It's warm and editorial but also confident. It won't disappear into the background. Make sure Davis and Alex are comfortable with 'SECOND' being the visual dominant — the name 'Second Chair' parsed as a display element puts 'SECOND' in a position that could read as either modest (second chair in a legal sense) or editorial (the second reading, the secondary perspective). Both readings are interesting. Neither is wrong. But the client should know which one they're choosing.*"

### Hegarty's Check

*"This is the direction with the most edge. And edge is usually where the right answer lives.*

*The brave choice here is the Burgundy 'CHAIR.' The client's instinct might be to set both words in the same color and let the size differential do the work. But the Burgundy on 'CHAIR' says something specific: 'CHAIR' is the institutional anchor. It's the part of the name that connects to the West Reporter, to the courtroom, to the credential. The color isn't decoration — it's meaning. Don't let anyone soften that.*

*The scalability note is real. If we go Direction C, we need a monogram mark that is as confident as the wordmark. The 'S' in Tiempos Bold alone is strong. But the standalone mark needs to earn its keep. I'd want to see it before committing.*

*Verdict on Direction C: It passes the bravery test — this is the most distinctive of the three. But it has the most execution dependencies. Get the monogram mark right. Test the single-color reduction. And make sure the client understands they're choosing the editorial register, not the plaque register.*"

## JON STEEL'S PSYCHOGRAPHIC CHECK

*"One thing I want to add before we close the presentation to Davis.*

*All three directions work strategically. But they each speak to a slightly different version of the attorney's identity.*

*Direction A (The Registry) speaks to the attorney who wants to feel like they belong to the institution. The wordmark says: this company is part of the same world as your law school, your federal court filings, your West Reporter volumes. It doesn't perform identity — it participates in it.*

*Direction B (The Plaque) speaks to the attorney who built their practice from a specific place — the partner's door, the lobby nameplate, the established firm. The engraved form says: this company has been here long enough to have a plate by the door. It's the mark of permanence.*

*Direction C (The Masthead) speaks to the attorney who came from BigLaw and chose PI work. The editorial register says: this company views the law the way the serious publications do — with analytical intelligence and respect for the institution. It's for the attorney who reads Law360 and the American Lawyer and privately wishes they had a better publication to read.*

*None of these is wrong. All three are authentic to segments of the PI attorney audience. The question for Davis is: which segment does he most want to speak to first? And which identity is he most authentically embodying as a founder?*"

## HEGARTY'S FINAL COMPARISON

*"Three strong directions. Let me make the comparison simple.*

**Direction A (The Registry):** The most timeless. The most restrained. The highest craft requirement. The one that will still look right in 20 years. Risk: it requires complete trust that the name alone is enough.

**Direction B (The Plaque):** The most versatile. The most functional — two-element system gives you the favicon, the embossing, the stamp. The most familiar to the professional services world. Risk: execution discipline must be iron-tight.

**Direction C (The Masthead):** The most distinctive. The most editorial. The one that most strongly signals 'we came from creative and we know how to read.' Risk: the most dependencies, the most complexity, the most room for things to go wrong in execution.

*If I were betting on which one builds the most distinctive brand over time: Direction A. Because it bets entirely on the name and the craft, and craft compounds. But if I were betting on which one is most immediately arresting visually — the one that stops someone scrolling — Direction C.*

*Direction B is the safe choice that isn't actually safe, because it's executed with genuine conviction.*

*My recommendation: Start in Figma with Direction A. Set 'SECOND CHAIR' in Century Schoolbook small caps on cream with the Burgundy rule. Live with it for 48 hours. Then set Direction C — 'SECOND' in Tiempos, 'CHAIR' in Caslon on the next line. See which one Davis cannot stop looking at. That's the answer.*"

## FIGMA BUILD SEQUENCE

When building these in Figma, work in this order:

1. Set up the color styles first (see `02_Color_System.md`) — cream as canvas, charcoal as text, burgundy as accent
2. Load the typefaces: Century Schoolbook Pro (or Century Schoolbook), Tiempos Headline Bold (Klim), Adobe Caslon Pro Regular
3. Build Direction A first — it's the typographic foundation for all three
4. Add the Burgundy rule as a separate rectangle element (not a text underline)
5. Build Direction B by adding the monogram mark to Direction A's wordmark
6. Build Direction C by replacing the wordmark with the two-typeface arrangement
7. Show all three at the same size on a cream canvas for direct comparison
8. Export at 3x for retina quality

**Typeface procurement note:** If Century Schoolbook Pro (with true OpenType small caps) is not yet available, use Adobe Caslon Pro for Direction A initially — it has true small caps and is itself a Track 2 typeface candidate. Do not use CSS `font-variant: small-caps` on regular capitals. The difference is visible.
