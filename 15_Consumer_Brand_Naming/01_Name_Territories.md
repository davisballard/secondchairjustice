## TERRITORY 1: JUSTICE / FAIRNESS

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"The temptation here is to reach for 'justice' as the big word. Don't. Maria Delgado doesn't say 'I want justice.' She says 'I just want to be fair about it.' She says 'I just want to be made whole.' Those are the words she uses when she's not performing — when she's talking to her husband, her sister, her coworker. 'Fair' is the word. 'Made whole' is the phrase. The name that lives in this territory uses their vocabulary, not ours."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Fair Claim** | "Fair" is the claimant's own word. "Claim" is the action. Two syllables each. |
| 2 | **Made Whole** | The exact phrase from claimant research. Legally meaningful, emotionally resonant. |
| 3 | **Fair Case** | "Case" instead of "claim" — lower litigation signal. "Fair" first. |
| 4 | **Square Deal** | Classic American idiom for fairness. Pre-litigant register. |
| 5 | **Fair Ground** | Even playing field. Neutral, balanced. "Fairground" as one word risks carnival connotation. |
| 6 | **Just Settlement** | "Just" as adjective (equitable), not noun (justice). |
| 7 | **Fair Settlement** | Direct. Uses "settlement" which has specific legal meaning — doubles as the outcome and the fairness. |
| 8 | **Even Case** | "Even" as in balanced, level. Less familiar idiom than Square Deal. |
| 9 | **Your Fair Claim** | Adds the possessive "your." |
| 10 | **Claim Fair** | Reversed compound. The fairness of the claim. |
| 11 | **True Claim** | "True" = honest + accurate + rightful. Three meanings in one word. |
| 12 | **Whole Claim** | "Whole" = complete + healthy + undivided. The claim you're actually owed. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"Fair Settlement and Just Settlement are phrases, not names — too many syllables for a brand that needs to be readable in 1.5 seconds on a mobile ad. Fair Ground has a carnival problem. Even Case doesn't land. Your Fair Claim is wordy. Claim Fair reverses the natural word order for no gain.*
>
> *Surviving: **Fair Claim** (their word, my action, clean), **Made Whole** (the exact phrase — no brand has this), **Square Deal** (the right idiom, though dated), **True Claim** (three meanings stacked in one word — that's craft)."*

**Craft-surviving T1:** Fair Claim, Made Whole, Square Deal, True Claim

## TERRITORY 2: KNOWLEDGE / INFORMATION

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"Maria's decision failure isn't anger or greed. It's ignorance. She does not know that the first offer is, on average, 23% of what she's owed. She does not know the gap. She does not know what she has. The moment she finds out — really finds out — her decision changes. So the name in this territory is about one thing: know before you decide. Not 'call us' — 'find out.' The action is information-seeking, which is identity-safe. You're not becoming a litigant. You're becoming informed."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Know Your Claim** | Direct. Empowering. Knowledge as permission. |
| 2 | **Claim Check** | Double meaning: "check on your claim" + "verify the facts." Audit your situation. |
| 3 | **First Look** | Before you commit, get a first look. Zero obligation frame. |
| 4 | **Claim Clarity** | What you get: clarity on your case. |
| 5 | **Case Value** | The thing you don't know: what your case is actually worth. |
| 6 | **Clear Claim** | Alliterative. Clear = transparent, honest, obvious, simple. |
| 7 | **Claim Sense** | Common sense applied to your claim. What the insurance company hopes you don't have. |
| 8 | **Case Truth** | The truth about your case, before you decide. |
| 9 | **Claim Facts** | The facts they aren't telling you. Harder edge. |
| 10 | **Know Before** | Before what? Needs context, but implies the stakes without stating them. |
| 11 | **Your Case Value** | Adding "your" — more possessive, more personal. |
| 12 | **Claim View** | Getting a view of your claim before acting. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"Claim Clarity sounds like a financial service's sub-brand. Case Value is transactional — 'value' in a brand name signals money-seeking, which triggers Maria's identity reflex. Case Truth is earnest to the point of feeling like a nonprofit slogan. Claim View is weak — 'view' suggests passive observation, not action.*
>
> *Surviving: **Know Your Claim** (the knowledge imperative stated clean), **Claim Check** (the double meaning earns its keep), **First Look** (minimum commitment, maximum honesty), **Clear Claim** (alliterative clarity that also implies honesty)."*

**Craft-surviving T2:** Know Your Claim, Claim Check, First Look, Clear Claim

## TERRITORY 3: ADVOCACY / BESIDE YOU

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"There's a specific failure mode in this territory: the brand sounds like a hotline. 'We're here for you.' 'You're not alone.' That's well-meaning and it's wrong, because it casts Maria as a victim — and she's already told us she doesn't want to see herself as a victim. She wants to see herself as someone making a smart, informed decision. So the advocacy in this territory has to be beside her, not above her. Not rescue — accompaniment."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Claim Ally** | Ally = beside you, not above you. Not rescue, partnership. |
| 2 | **Your Corner** | "In your corner" — universal support idiom without aggression. |
| 3 | **Claim Guide** | Navigation through a process, not rescue from a crisis. |
| 4 | **First Counsel** | "Counsel" as advice, not attorney. The guidance before the decision. |
| 5 | **Case Ally** | Same as Claim Ally with "case" instead of "claim." |
| 6 | **Claim Side** | On your side. Simple. |
| 7 | **Your Witness** | A witness to your situation. Someone who sees what happened. |
| 8 | **Standing Claim** | Where you stand. What you have standing to do. |
| 9 | **Claim Advocate** | Direct but "advocate" can sound organizational/nonprofit. |
| 10 | **Case Companion** | Alliterative, but "companion" reads too soft. |
| 11 | **Claim Partner** | Partnership frame. "Partner" is overused in B2B. |
| 12 | **True Advocate** | "True" qualifies the advocacy — honest advocacy, not performance. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"First Counsel sounds like the name of a law firm's newsletter. Case Ally is Claim Ally with worse sound — 'case' and 'ally' don't have the same rhythm as 'claim' and 'ally.' Claim Side is weak, needs 'your' to work. Your Witness is interesting but risks the legal-proceeding connotation. Case Companion is too soft. Claim Partner sounds B2B. True Advocate is a nonprofit with an adjective.*
>
> *Surviving: **Claim Ally** (the warmth without the weakness), **Your Corner** (the universal idiom of support), **Claim Guide** (navigation not rescue — exactly the right frame for DeShawn), **Your Witness** (keeping it: 'witness' is both legally significant and emotionally resonant — someone who sees what happened to you)."*

**Craft-surviving T3:** Claim Ally, Your Corner, Claim Guide, Your Witness

## TERRITORY 4: RESTORATION / WHOLENESS

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"The claimant's deepest stated want is not money. It's return. 'I just want things to go back to normal.' 'I just want to be made whole.' The legal doctrine of 'made whole' captures this exactly: full restoration to the position you were in before someone else's negligence changed it. The name in this territory isn't about getting rich. It's about getting back. The danger: this sounds like a wellness app or a rehab clinic. Needs to stay anchored to the claim context."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Claim Recovery** | Recovery = health recovery + financial recovery. The accident victim is doing both. |
| 2 | **Restore Claim** | Action frame: restore what's yours. |
| 3 | **Whole Case** | "Whole" = complete, fully recovered, what you're owed. |
| 4 | **Back To Whole** | Phrase, too long for a brand name. But the sentiment is exact. |
| 5 | **Full Claim** | What you're owed in full. Complete recovery of what's yours. |
| 6 | **Recovery Claim** | Reversed — emphasizes recovery first. |
| 7 | **Claim Restored** | Past tense creates a completeness feeling. |
| 8 | **Whole Recovery** | Two wholeness words — redundant register. |
| 9 | **True Recovery** | "True" again — the real recovery, not the inadequate settlement. |
| 10 | **Restore Case** | Restoration of your case's value. |
| 11 | **What's Yours** | Very direct. "Get what's yours." Possessive without aggression. |
| 12 | **Yours Fully** | Awkward. What's yours, fully recovered. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"Back To Whole is a phrase, not a brand name. Whole Recovery stacks two wholeness words into redundancy. Recovery Claim reverses into awkward. Restore Claim verb-noun works but 'restore' reads wellness. Claim Restored is past tense, which is odd for a brand promising future outcomes. Yours Fully is grammatically strained.*
>
> *Surviving: **Claim Recovery** (the health/financial double meaning earns the name), **Full Claim** (what you're owed in full — no ambiguity, no sentimentality), **What's Yours** (the most direct possessive in the exercise — risky but honest), **True Recovery** ('true' qualifies the recovery as the real one, not the insurance company's approximation)."*

**Craft-surviving T4:** Claim Recovery, Full Claim, What's Yours, True Recovery

## TERRITORY 5: TIME / MOMENT

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"The 72-hour window is the most consequential strategic fact in the claimant's experience. Most of the decisions that determine their outcome are made — or made for them — in the first 72 hours. The insurance adjuster knows this. The advertising that serves the claimant honestly puts a name on this moment: before you decide, before you sign, before you say yes. The danger is urgency manipulation — 'Act Now,' 'Limited Time.' That's the adjuster's playbook, not ours. The time reference here is honest: 'before you commit.' Not pressure. Awareness."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Before You Sign** | The exact critical moment. The most specific honest warning in the exercise. |
| 2 | **First Call** | The minimum viable action as a brand name. |
| 3 | **One Call First** | The whole B2C strategy in three words. |
| 4 | **Before You Decide** | Slightly softer than Before You Sign. Less specific moment. |
| 5 | **Claim First** | Your claim, first — before anything else. |
| 6 | **First Step Claim** | The first step is a claim review. Too many words. |
| 7 | **Right Moment** | Vague. The right moment for what? |
| 8 | **Claim Window** | The 72-hour window. Opaque without explanation. |
| 9 | **One Call** | Shorter than First Call. More command-like. |
| 10 | **First Choice** | You have a first choice before you make the insurance company's choice. Interesting. |
| 11 | **Before You Know** | Before you know what you have. Slightly ominous. |
| 12 | **Know First** | Know what you have first. Clean imperative. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"First Step Claim is too many words and reads like a process diagram. Right Moment is a search keyword not a brand. Claim Window requires the explanation about 72 hours that you never get to give in an ad. Before You Know has an ominous undertone. Before You Decide is a weaker version of Before You Sign — less specific moment, less honest. First Choice is interesting but 'choice' implies a binary we're not sure we want to imply.*
>
> *Surviving: **Before You Sign** (the bravest name in the exercise — naming the exact moment of danger), **First Call** (minimum viable action as brand — this is the whole instruction in two words), **One Call First** (three words that describe the B2C positioning strategy exactly), **Know First** (the imperative — find out before you commit)."*

**Craft-surviving T5:** Before You Sign, First Call, One Call First, Know First

## TERRITORY 6: PLAIN LANGUAGE

**Jon Steel frames the territory:**

*"The accident victim in the first 72 hours does not have cognitive bandwidth for cleverness. They are in pain, in overwhelm, managing four simultaneous crises. The name that reaches them in that state is a name that asks nothing of them — no decoding, no metaphor, no second reading. It says what it is. The risk: too plain becomes too generic. There's a narrow space between obvious and invisible."*

**Ed McCabe — Raw Generation:**

| # | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Your Claim** | The simplest possessive. It's yours. |
| 2 | **Claim Review** | The thing they're offering. A review before commitment. |
| 3 | **Honest Claim** | Second Chair's core positioning word applied to the consumer brand. |
| 4 | **Accident Claim** | Literal. Also the search query. Very SEO. |
| 5 | **Claim Help** | What it does. Simple. Help. |
| 6 | **Case Review** | Same as Claim Review with "case." |
| 7 | **Free Claim Review** | Product feature as brand name — sounds like a homepage CTA. |
| 8 | **Your Case** | Same as Your Claim with "case." |
| 9 | **Claim Plain** | The meta-naming of the territory. Awkward word order. |
| 10 | **Plain Claim** | Reversed. Slightly better. Still awkward. |
| 11 | **True Case** | Honest register. Your true case value. |
| 12 | **Right Claim** | "Right" = rightful + correct. What you're rightfully owed. |

**Ed McCabe — Craft Filter (cuts to 4):**

> *"Accident Claim is the search query, not the brand — it's a keyword strategy not an identity. Free Claim Review is a button label. Case Review and Claim Review are the same idea, Claim Review has slightly better rhythm because 'claim' is the emotional word. Claim Plain and Plain Claim both have word-order problems. Claim Help is fine but 'help' as the second word creates a welfare register we're trying to avoid.*
>
> *Surviving: **Your Claim** (the minimum honest possession — this is yours), **Claim Review** (the honest product feature as name — a review before deciding), **Honest Claim** (Second Chair's positioning word in the consumer brand — the most direct expression of the differentiation), **Right Claim** ('right' stacks rightful, correct, and fair in one syllable)."*

**Craft-surviving T6:** Your Claim, Claim Review, Honest Claim, Right Claim

## NIGEL'S CLOSING NOTE

*"Eleven names for Davis. They're real names — not slogans dressed up as brands. Each represents a different angle on the same strategic truth: this is the brand that tells accident victims what the insurance company won't.*

*My read on the strongest positions:*

*If Davis wants the emotional territory — the name that lives in the claimant's vocabulary before they even know the brand exists — it's **Made Whole**. That's the name they already use for what they want.*

*If he wants the minimum viable action named — the thing we're literally asking them to do — it's **First Call**. That's the instruction and the brand in two syllables.*

*If he wants the bravest choice — the name that stops someone mid-scroll because it sounds like it's talking directly to them — it's **Before You Sign**.*

*These are four genuinely different directions. The others are strong alternatives.*

*Davis decides."*
