# Second Chair — B2C Consumer Brand Naming Session Brief
*Session Date: March 1, 2026*

---

## THE PROBLEM

Second Chair runs B2C consumer advertising to attract accident victims (PI claimants) and connect them with PI attorneys. That advertising runs under a white-labeled brand — not the Second Chair name, which is B2B and attorney-facing.

The current white-label brand name is **Claim Justice Now**. It cannot be used. It is too close to a competitor's existing brand. The name is off the table.

Davis wants to replace it. He likes elements of what "Claim Justice Now" was doing — invoking something, claiming what's yours, the directness of "now." But he is open to moving beyond the justice territory if the right alternative emerges.

This session produces a shortlist of viable B2C brand names for Davis to evaluate and ultimately select from.

---

## THE ROOM

**Nigel Bogle** — Account Management. Opens the session, moderates, keeps the exercise commercially grounded. Nothing that can't survive the business test.

**Jon Steel** — Narrative Strategy. Owns the consumer psychographic. Every name gets evaluated through one lens: *"Does this give Maria permission to call without feeling like a litigant?"* Jon does not generate names. He evaluates them.

**Ed McCabe** — Senior Copywriter. Territory generation and word-level craft. He generates the raw candidates across six naming territories and performs the first craft filter. His frameworks: Slaughter (PI victim psychology), Collier (empathy and permission), Caples (clarity and directness).

**John Hegarty** — Executive Creative Director. Final bravery and quality check. He cuts anything that performs rather than embodies. Anything that's costume. Anything that tries too hard or too little.

---

## WHAT THIS NAME IS DOING

The B2C brand appears on:
- Meta/Instagram/Facebook ads targeting accident victims
- Google display and search ads (72-hour post-accident targeting window)
- Landing pages claimants arrive on after clicking an ad
- The phone number they call and the confirmation message they receive
- Potentially: pre-accident brand awareness ads (the 60% pre-accident spend)

The name is **not** Second Chair. It is a separate, standalone brand that accident victims encounter without necessarily knowing it's connected to the attorney marketing company behind it. The white-label design is intentional — it allows the brand to feel like an independent consumer resource, not a vendor sales tool.

## THE CONSUMER INSIGHT ANCHOR

*(Jon Steel — required reading before name generation begins)*

From the claimant research (`Claimant_Profile.md`, February 2026):

### Who Is in the Room

The modal accident victim who will see this brand is:
- Working-class or middle-class, approximately 40 years old
- Employed, financially stressed without cushion
- In physical pain and cognitive overwhelm in the first 72 hours
- Under active, deliberate pressure from an adjuster trained to minimize their claim
- Carrying a deep identity barrier: **"I'm not the kind of person who sues"**

They are not thinking about justice. They are thinking about getting back to their life.

### What They Say

The language accident victims use reveals what actually moves them:

- **"I just want to be fair about it."** — Not "I want to fight." Not "I want millions." Fair. That's the word.
- **"I just want to be made whole."** — Restoration, not victory. Getting back to where they were.
- **"I don't want to make a big deal of it."** — The identity barrier in language form. This is the thing the name cannot trigger.
- **"It costs nothing to find out, right?"** — The permission structure that works. Information-seeking, not commitment-making.
- **"I wish I had known."** — The 12-month regret. The truth the advertising is working against.

### What They Need the Name to Do

The name is not calling them to battle. It is not promising them millions. It is not invoking their anger.

The name is giving them **one permission**: *before you decide, find out what you have.*

A name that feels like a legal firm is wrong. A name that feels like a class action settlement notice is wrong. A name that feels like an aggressive billboard is wrong.

The name that works feels like: *a resource. An honest one. Someone who will tell you the truth before you make a decision you can't unmake.*

### The $33,700 Truth

The data is this: accident victims who accept the first offer without consulting an attorney get, on average, $33,700 less than those who do consult one. 68% accept the first offer. 91% of those later find the compensation was insufficient.

This is the factual foundation the brand exists to address. The name does not have to say this. But it should not contradict it.

## WHAT THE NAME MUST BE

**Legally clean** — Not too close to any existing PI lead gen brand. "ClaimJustice," "AccidentHelp," "LegalMatch," and their variants are off the table.

**Identity-safe** — Cannot signal "lawsuit," "litigation," "fighting," or "getting rich." The claimant who hears it should not feel like they're crossing a line into becoming a litigant.

**Honest in register** — Consistent with Second Chair's core positioning: honest consumer advertising. The name cannot sound like the ambulance-chaser register the category runs on. No: "CashForCrash," "InjuryPros," "AccidentCash," "CallNow."

**Actionable without urgency manipulation** — Can suggest a moment or a step. Cannot manipulate with fake urgency. "Now" as a hard close is off. "Before you decide" as a genuine invite is fine.

**URL-viable** — Should be available or acquirable as a .com. Short enough to say over the phone. Spell-able by someone in pain filling out a form on a phone.

**Standalone** — Works without explanation. A Maria Delgado who's never heard of Second Chair should see it and understand, at minimum: *this is something that exists to help people in my situation.*

---

## FILTERING CRITERIA (in order)

1. **Craft filter (Ed McCabe):** Does it have rhythm? Is it clear on first read? Does it pass the "say it aloud twice" test — does it still feel right the second time? Cut anything that requires explanation or sounds like a tagline.

2. **Psychographic filter (Jon Steel):** Does it give Maria permission to call without triggering her identity barrier? Does it feel like a resource or a law firm? Does it sound like the honest side of the category, or the dishonest side?

3. **Bravery check (John Hegarty):** Does it feel like it could belong to any brand in any category, or does it feel distinctly right for this? Is it doing something, or is it just not doing anything wrong? The name must earn its existence.

## TERRITORY MAP

Six naming territories. Ed McCabe generates across all six. Davis may end up selecting from one territory, multiple territories, or somewhere between them.

The territories are:

| # | Territory | Core Idea | Danger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Justice / Fairness | "Fair" and "made whole" — what claimants say, not what lawyers say | Landing in the competitor zone or the lofty-justice zone |
| 2 | Knowledge / Information | Knowing what you have before you decide | Sounds too passive or consultancy-like |
| 3 | Advocacy / Beside You | Someone in your corner, without the aggression | Sounds like a nonprofit or a hotline |
| 4 | Restoration / Wholeness | Getting back to where you were | Sounds like a wellness brand |
| 5 | Time / Moment | Before you sign, the timing matters | Tips into urgency manipulation |
| 6 | Plain Language | Naming what's actually happening, simply | Too blunt or too generic |
