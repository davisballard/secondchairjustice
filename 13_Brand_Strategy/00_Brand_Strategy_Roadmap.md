## On Sequencing: Proceed Without Alex First

**Start strategy sessions now. Sync with Alex after the Creative Brief is locked — then let his response reshape the product layer.**

This is not a sequencing decision that diminishes Alex's contribution. It is the opposite: Alex's creative and product intelligence is too important to be filtered through a brief that is still forming. The right moment for Alex to engage is when there is something specific in front of him — a locked creative direction, a defined positioning, a set of product implications — that he can respond to, expand, and make real.

The correct sequence:

1. Davis (+ the agency team) creates the strategy through Sessions 0–4 and locks the Creative Brief
2. Alex reads the locked brief and responds as a full creative and strategic partner from the product lens: what he sees in it, what it opens up that Davis couldn't have seen, what he'd build to fulfill it and how, and what new ideas it triggers
3. Alex's response adds a second dimension to the strategy — the product layer that makes the positioning defensible, specific, and genuinely buildable at a level the category cannot match
4. The creative brief may be updated to reflect what Alex's product thinking unlocks — not reopened, but sharpened

Running strategy sessions while waiting for Alex to be "in the room" would produce a strategy negotiated against execution reality rather than one built at full ambition. Davis sets the strategic and creative direction first. Then Alex responds to it — and his response will change what Second Chair can actually claim to be.

Alex's role in the strategy sessions, in his absence: every territory and positioning decision should assume "Alex can build whatever this requires." His response to the locked brief is where his creative and strategic contribution formally enters — and it will be one of the most important moments in building this brand.

### The Non-Negotiable Filter for All Territories

Before generating territories, apply this filter. Any territory that fails any of these tests is eliminated before it goes to Session 2:

1. **The fusion test:** Does this territory require BOTH Davis's advertising/creative capability AND Alex's product/engineering capability to be credible? A territory that only requires great advertising — and could be delivered by hiring a good agency — fails this test. A territory that only requires a good product team — and doesn't need advertising excellence — fails this test. Second Chair's differentiator is the combination. The territory must be impossible to claim without both.
2. **The credential test:** Does this territory require the specific capabilities these founders bring — or could a well-funded generic lead gen operator claim it with enough money and a good agency hire? If any competitor with $10M in capital and the right hires could claim it within 18 months, it is not the right territory for Second Chair.
3. **The justice test:** Does this territory reflect a genuine point of view about what the advertising supply chain owes the people inside it — attorneys and accident victims — or is it a commercial claim dressed in ethical language? If it is the latter, it does not survive.
4. **The expansion test:** Does this territory work for PI MVA and also have room to grow into other high-CPL legal verticals (mass tort, SSD, worker's comp) without requiring a repositioning? A territory that only makes sense for MVA is too narrow. Second Chair's model is a proof of concept for a broader category transformation.
5. **The "only us" test (Hegarty's standard):** Could any other company in any other market say this? If yes, keep refining. The winning territory is the one that is impossible to say without the specific team, background, and belief system Second Chair has.

### A Critical Note on the Sales Motion (Multi-Vendor Reality)

Before territory descriptions: Second Chair enters attorney accounts as an **additional vendor, not a replacement.** PI attorneys run multiple lead gen vendors simultaneously and routinely add new ones without dropping existing relationships. The sales motion is "try us alongside what you're already running" — attorneys allocate budget across multiple vendors and expand when they find one that performs.

The evidence: firms like Jacoby & Meyers will start with 50 leads from any semi-legitimate vendor just to test. There is no switching cost, no lock-in, no zero-sum displacement. Month-to-month terms support this — an attorney adding Second Chair is making an allocation decision, not a commitment decision.

This changes the framing of all territory descriptions and all subsequent sales materials. The question is never "why switch to us" — it is "why add us." The barrier is lower (no displacement required), the test is accessible (50 leads to start), and the proof of concept is self-evident (CPSC data after the first month of signed cases). Territories should be framed as a compelling reason to allocate a test budget alongside existing vendors — not as arguments for replacing the vendor an attorney currently uses.

Alex's precision digital infrastructure (geo-targeting, behavioral intake, sub-60-second speed-to-lead, compliant contact protocols at FINRA-grade reliability) is the delivery system that captures the moment before the adjuster's frame is established.

*What makes this "only us":* This territory is both an advertising claim (honest, specific advertising is more effective in the 72-hour window because it's not trying to manage inflated expectations) and a product claim (the intake and routing infrastructure built to operate at this specificity of timing). ## Session 2 — Territory Debate and Selection

**Folder:** `02_Territory_Exploration/`
**Deliverable:** `02_Territory_Exploration/Territory_Decision.md`

### Purpose

The full agency round table. Every territory is argued, challenged, defended. The session ends with one selected territory and a locked single-minded proposition. No ambiguity leaving this room.

### Who Is In the Room

Full agency. Every agent who participated in Session 1, plus:
- All strategy agents (for commercial and effectiveness challenge)
- All creative agents (for brave-enough test)
- Richard Roma (for sales-reality final check)
- Nigel Bogle (for commercial final check)

Julian Cole chairs. Jon Steel co-chairs (his role: challenge any territory that doesn't have a real human story inside it).

### How the Round Table Runs

**Round 1 — Territory Defense**
Each territory gets a 3-minute defense. Julian or the designated champion makes the case for why this is the right territory. No hedging. Make the strongest possible case.

**Round 2 — Targeted Challenges**
Each agent challenges each territory through their specific lens. The challenges are not general — they are specific to what the agent has found in the research that makes this territory risky or unconvincing.

**Round 3 — Synthesis**
After challenges, the group identifies: which territories survive intact? Which can be merged? Which are definitively eliminated and why?

**Round 4 — Selection and Lock**
One territory is selected. If there is genuine disagreement, Nigel Bogle's commercial test and Hegarty's creative test are the tiebreakers. A territory that can be sold and that produces brave creative work wins over a territory that is strategically elegant but commercially or creatively inert.

**Folder:** `03_Brand_Architecture/`
**Deliverable:** `03_Brand_Architecture/Brand_Architecture.md`

### Purpose

Turn the selected territory and locked SMP into a complete brand structure. Everything Second Chair will ever say or do in the market flows from this document. It is the brand's constitution.

### Who Is In the Room

| Agent | Role |
|-------|------|

| Nigel Bogle | Commercial review — will this architecture close deals? |
| John Hegarty | Creative mandate review — is the brief that will come from this brave enough? |

### What the Architecture Covers

**Section 1: Positioning Statement (locked)**
The complete positioning statement in the format established in the research:
- For whom / Who find that / [Brand] is / Because / So that
- This is the strategy team's version — internal. Not advertising copy.

**Section 2: Single-Minded Proposition (locked)**
One sentence. The entire brand strategy compressed. The test: if you forgot everything else in this document, the SMP alone would still point you in the right direction.

**Section 3: B2B Message Architecture**

The hierarchy of messages for PI attorneys:

```
Brand Belief (the one thing — never changes)
  ↓
Primary Message (what attorneys lead with in their decision)
  ↓
Supporting Messages (3 max — proof points in priority order)
  ↓
Objection Responses (pre-mapped to the 5 most common attorney objections)
```

Reference: `07_Research/01_Brand_Strategy_Research/01_Consumer_Deep_Dive/05_Attorney_Marketing_World/Attorney_Buying_Voice.md` for the language attorneys actually use when deciding.

**Section 4: B2C Message Architecture**

The hierarchy of messages for accident victims:

```
Recognition Message (the moment we enter — the 72-hour window)
  ↓
Evidence Message (the specific data that creates belief)
  ↓
Action Message (the minimum viable ask)
```

Reference: `07_Research/01_Brand_Strategy_Research/01_Consumer_Deep_Dive/06_B2C_Claimant_Consumer/Claimant_Decision_Architecture.md` for what triggers action.

**Section 5: Brand Character**

3–4 character traits. Each defined by what it IS and what it IS NOT. The "is not" definition is more useful than the "is" — it prevents the character from collapsing into generic brand language.

Format for each trait:
```
[Trait Name]
Is: [Specific, behavioral definition]
Is not: [The easy version that doesn't count]
Sounds like: [One example line]
Does not sound like: [One line that violates this trait]
```

**Section 6: Tone of Voice**

Written by Ed McCabe, reviewed by Jon Steel. Not a list of adjectives. A set of principles with real examples drawn from the research's "private language" findings.

Must address:
- The register for B2B (attorney-facing) communication
- The register for B2C (claimant-facing) communication
- What Second Chair never says, and why

Reference: `07_Research/01_Brand_Strategy_Research/01_Consumer_Deep_Dive/07_Strategic_Implications/Ed_McCabe_Consumer_Analysis.md`

**Section 7: Proof Points**

The verifiable evidence that makes every brand claim credible. For pre-launch: specific, concrete facts about the product, business model, and team that can be stated without qualification. For post-launch: performance data format to be populated.

Each proof point must pass the "specific and verifiable" test. "We care about quality" is not a proof point. "Our advertising discloses that attorney advertising is paid — which no competitor does" is a proof point.

**Section 8: What Second Chair Never Does**

The brand's non-negotiables. Behaviors, claims, tactics, and creative approaches that are permanently off-limits regardless of competitive pressure or revenue opportunity. This section is the brand's moral backbone. It must be specific enough to be actionable.
